# Skill: Respondent Response to Emergency Interim Measures Application

## 1. Subject-matter triage

- Determine at the outset whether the application is for emergency relief, tribunal-issued interim measures, or seat-court provisional relief, because the applicable standards and timing differ.
- Identify whether the application is a single-issue request or a bundle of requests; if multiple forms of relief are sought, analyze each separately before synthesizing priority.
- Confirm the governing arbitration framework, seat-based law, institutional rules, and any emergency arbitrator provisions before assessing merits.
- If the source materials include multiple financial periods, counterparties, asset classes, or requested measures, enumerate them first and analyze each on its own record.

## 2. Failure modes the skill is correcting

- Accepts the applicant's financial assertions at face value without verifying them against the underlying agreement, payment records, and supporting materials, missing inflated or unsupported amounts that may overstate the claimed entitlement
- Does not assess the requested freeze or attachment as a proportion of the respondent's liquid assets, leaving the proportionality objection without a quantitative basis
- Fails to identify alternative arrangements the applicant has made that may mitigate the claimed harm, undermining the irreparable-harm analysis
- Does not flag the absence of a required cross-undertaking or similar damages protection as a procedural deficiency in the application
- Treats delay, jurisdiction, or procedural defects as background points instead of threshold objections that may dispose of the application
- Collapses distinct interim-measures tests into one generalized fairness argument, losing the separate analyses for urgency, necessity, proportionality, and harm

## 3. Legal frameworks / domain conventions that apply

- Emergency relief framework: apply the governing institutional rule set for emergency arbitrator or interim-measures relief, together with any seat-based arbitration law governing provisional measures and judicial assistance.
- Prima facie jurisdiction: test standing, the existence and scope of the arbitration agreement, and whether the requested relief falls within the arbitral tribunal's provisional powers.
- Irreparable-harm standard: identify whether the alleged injury is truly non-compensable or whether monetary damages, contractual remedies, insurance, escrow, setoff, or other arrangements undercut the claim.
- Urgency and delay: compare the timing of the triggering event, notice, negotiations, and filing date; unexplained delay weakens an emergency showing under most arbitration rules and seat laws.
- Proportionality and necessity: measure the breadth of the requested restraint against the claimed exposure, the respondent's liquidity, and the practical effect of the order; ask whether a narrower measure would suffice.
- Cross-undertaking or security for damages: many arbitration frameworks and emergency-practice orders require protection if relief later proves unwarranted; if the application omits it, identify the gap and its consequence.
- Evidentiary verification: financial assertions, ownership claims, asset descriptions, and causal narratives should be checked against the source set, and unsupported assumptions should be called out expressly.
- Controlling authority: when stating any legal proposition, cite the governing rule, treaty article, institutional provision, statute, or leading authority that supplies it.

## 4. Analytical scaffolds

- Start with a threshold screen: jurisdiction, admissibility, timeliness, and procedural compliance.
- Then test each requested measure against the operative interim-relief elements: urgency, necessity, irreparable harm, proportionality, and preservation of the status quo.
- Verify every financial figure against the underlying agreement, payment history, notices, accounting records, or expert material; identify any unsupported, stale, duplicated, or overstated amount.
- If an expert or damages schedule is provided, check the internal arithmetic and any mismatch between assumptions and supporting documents.
- Measure any freeze, attachment, or asset restraint against the respondent's liquid assets and identify the practical operational impact of the restraint.
- Identify any alternative arrangements, substitute security, escrow, guarantees, setoff rights, or mitigating conduct that weaken the claimed emergency.
- Assess whether the application seeks relief broader than the harm alleged, and whether a narrower order would address the asserted risk.
- Determine whether the application contains the required procedural protections, including any cross-undertaking, security, or return-date mechanisms.
- For each issue, state the legal rule, the record support, the scale of the problem, the interaction with another source document, and the client consequence.

## 5. Vertical / structural / temporal relationships

- Track the chronology from the underlying dispute trigger through notice, exchange of correspondence, escalation, filing, and requested hearing date.
- Compare the timing of alleged harm to the timing of the requested order; a long gap, intervening negotiation, or parallel arrangement may defeat urgency.
- If the application is based on multiple accounts, projects, or obligations, map how each item relates to the requested measure so the response targets the weakest linkage first.
- Identify cross-document dependencies: the application, arbitration agreement, interim-relief rule, financial records, and any emergency-order requirements should be read together, not in isolation.
- When multiple requested restraints are presented, prioritize the one with the greatest operational or liquidity impact and evaluate whether the others are duplicative.

## 6. Output structure conventions

- Write a respondent-facing issues memorandum, not a brief on the merits.
- Open with a concise executive summary that ranks the strongest objections in priority order.
- Include a defined severity scale at the top and apply it uniformly to every issue entry.
- For each issue, use a compact format that includes: issue title, severity, governing authority, source support, scale of the defect, interaction with another document or clause, and downstream consequence for the respondent.
- Separate threshold objections from merits objections, and keep procedural defects distinct from evidentiary defects.
- Include a financial verification section that lists each challenged figure, the record-supported figure, and the discrepancy or uncertainty.
- Include a proportionality analysis that compares the requested restraint to the respondent's liquid resources and practical business effect.
- Include a section on irreparable harm and urgency that addresses alternative arrangements and timing.
- Include a legal framework section identifying the governing emergency-relief regime and any seat-based law that matters.
- End with an explicit Recommended Actions block. Each recommendation must use an imperative verb, identify the responsible role, and anchor timing to a concrete deadline or to the next procedural milestone.
- Keep the tone practical and litigation-focused; the memo should help counsel decide what to oppose first and what evidence to gather next.
