# Skill: Procedural Timetable vs. Institutional Rules Compliance Review

## 1. Subject-matter triage (only if applicable)

- Identify the governing institutional rules edition from the arbitration commencement date and any transition language; do not assume the procedural order date controls.
- If the source set includes multiple procedural orders, directions, or annexed timetables, enumerate each item first and compare each against the same governing rules baseline unless the documents themselves identify different phases or instruments.
- Confirm the dispute-management posture: this is a respondent-oriented deviation review, so prioritize objections that can be raised efficiently and preserve later procedural rights.
- Use the attached case documents only to resolve timing, party setup, logistics, consent, and citation questions that the procedural order itself leaves unclear.

## 2. Failure modes the skill is correcting

- Applies the wrong edition of the institutional arbitration rules; the comparison must be anchored to the governing version, not the issuance date of the procedural order.
- Misses a mandatory early-case procedural step or deadline that the rules require the timetable to address.
- Overlooks whether a tribunal secretary or similar appointee is confined to administration or is being asked to perform substantive work that may require party consent under the applicable practice.
- Checks substance but fails to verify whether article references in the order map to the correct provisions in the governing edition.
- Treats an objection as complete when it merely identifies a mismatch, without tying that mismatch to the source documents and the practical prejudice to the respondent.
- Collapses several timing, logistics, and citation problems into one generic criticism instead of separating distinct deviations.

## 3. Legal frameworks / domain conventions that apply

- Institutional rules edition selection: determine the applicable version by commencement date and any transition provisions; article numbering and deadlines may differ between editions, so every comparison must use the controlling edition.
- Mandatory case-management step: identify whether the rules require an early procedural action or scheduling item within a prescribed period, then test whether the order includes it and whether the timing is workable.
- Tribunal secretary / administrative assistant practice: separate purely administrative support from substantive drafting or decision-making support; if the latter is implicated, assess whether consent is needed under the applicable institutional practice and whether the record reflects consent.
- Final-award timing and scheduling logic: verify that the timetable leaves room for any rules-based period for closing submissions, deliberation, and issuance of the award under the governing edition.
- Hearing and witness logistics: if the case setup suggests remote participation, travel, interpreter use, or witness sequencing issues, confirm the order addresses them with enough specificity to avoid avoidable delay or due-process friction.
- Cross-reference discipline: every cited rule, article, or appendix should be checked against the governing rules edition and any internal cross-reference chain in the procedural order.

## 4. Analytical scaffolds

- Establish the governing rules edition first, then verify whether the procedural order expressly cites that edition and whether any cited article numbers align with that version.
- Build a deviation register, not a narrative only: each entry should isolate one distinct issue, identify the controlling rule or practice point, and state whether the order complies, partially complies, or deviates.
- For each deviation, do all three:
  - anchor the point to a specific threshold, deadline, milestone, or scheduling feature appearing in the source set;
  - cross-reference the related clause, annex, schedule, or prior order that interacts with it;
  - explain the concrete consequence for the respondent, such as loss of procedural clarity, compressed preparation time, consent risk, or later objection preservation.
- Treat an issue as incomplete if it stops at “inconsistent” or “incorrect” without identifying the governing authority, the interacting document, and the downstream procedural effect.
- Assign one ordinal severity label to each entry using a stated scale: Critical / High / Medium / Low.
  - Critical: creates a serious due-process, jurisdictional, or timetable defect that should be raised immediately.
  - High: materially prejudices respondent preparation, hearing fairness, or rule compliance.
  - Medium: non-trivial deviation requiring correction or clarification.
  - Low: technical or drafting issue with limited practical impact.
- If only one rules edition is plausibly in scope, say so affirmatively and explain why; do not speculate across multiple editions without a source basis.
- Cite the controlling authority for every legal or institutional proposition relied on, including rule articles, transition provisions, and any practice standard used to assess secretary consent or timetable sufficiency.

## 5. Vertical / structural / temporal relationships (only if applicable)

- Map the timetable chronologically: commencement, pleadings, procedural conference, document production, witness evidence, hearing, post-hearing steps, deliberation, and award issuance.
- Check whether each date or period leaves coherent space for the preceding and following steps; flag compressed or overlapping periods that make a downstream step unrealistic.
- Where a procedure depends on an earlier event, verify the dependency chain is reflected in the order and any incorporated schedule.
- If the order incorporates multiple annexes or schedules, verify that the cross-references between the main order and annexes are internally consistent and complete.
- If witness, interpreter, or remote-hearing logistics are addressed, confirm the order allocates responsibility and timing for those logistics rather than assuming they will self-execute.
- If a tribunal secretary role is described, identify whether the timetable channels substantive drafting or analysis through that role and whether the consent posture is documented.

## 6. Output structure conventions

- Produce a respondent-oriented compliance deviation report, suitable for a .docx deliverable, with the governing rules edition stated at the outset.
- Start with a short rules-edition analysis: which edition applies, how it was determined, and whether the order cites the correct version.
- Include a severity key once, then an issue register with one entry per deviation; keep each entry discrete and ordered by importance.
- For each issue entry, include:
  - severity;
  - the challenged timetable feature or citation;
  - the controlling rule or practice authority;
  - the interacting source document or cross-reference, if any;
  - the prejudice or practical consequence for respondent;
  - a concise objection framing.
- Include a cross-reference verification table that lists each article citation or rules reference appearing in the order, whether it is correct, and the correct reference if not.
- End with prioritized objections the respondent should raise, phrased as tribunal-facing requests or reservations rather than pure commentary.
- Include an explicit Recommended Actions block at the end, using imperative verbs, a responsible role, and a timing anchor tied to the next procedural milestone or filing deadline.
- Keep the tone precise, restrained, and advocacy-aware; avoid overclaiming where the source set supports only a clarification request or partial objection.
