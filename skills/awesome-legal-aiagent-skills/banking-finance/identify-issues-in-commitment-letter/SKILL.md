# Skill: Commitment Letter Issues Memo — Sponsor Perspective

## 1. Subject-matter triage
- Treat the commitment letter package, merger agreement summary, and related engagement materials as a single financing record.
- Identify whether the package is a single-transaction sponsor buyout, a dual-track financing, or a variant with multiple committed tranches; if multiple financing pieces exist, analyze each separately before comparing them.
- Read from the sponsor’s perspective: the question is not whether the lender has drafted a market-standard letter in the abstract, but whether the sponsor can reliably close on the acquisition on the contemplated timetable and terms.
- If the package includes amendments, term sheets, fee letters, or slides, reconcile them against the main commitment letter rather than reading each in isolation.

## 2. Failure modes the skill is correcting
- Missing a mismatch between commitment duration and the financing process timeline, which can leave the sponsor exposed before syndication or closing is complete.
- Treating broad market-out language as routine when sponsor financings generally require tighter, target-specific or closing-specific conditioning.
- Collapsing flex analysis into pricing only, and failing to isolate covenant flex as a separate borrower-side risk.
- Overlooking extra funding conditions that exceed the customary limited-condition framework for acquisition financings.
- Missing scope creep in collateral perfection, expense reimbursement, clear market, or indemnity provisions that undermines commitment certainty.
- Failing to rank issues by practical closing risk, which makes the memo less useful for negotiating priorities.

## 3. Legal frameworks / domain conventions that apply
- Commitment certainty: compare the stated commitment term, outside dates, and any extension mechanics to the expected closing and syndication path; a commitment that can lapse before the deal can reasonably fund is a material sponsor-side risk.
- Limited-condition acquisition financing practice: the funding conditions should track customary acquisition-financing closing conditions, with deviations called out separately and specifically.
- Market-out and market-condition concepts: assess whether any market-condition funding condition is narrow and transaction-specific or broad enough to function as a lender walk-away right.
- Flex mechanics: review pricing, original issue discount, maturity, collateral package, yield, and covenant flex together; a flex package without a real constraint on use can materially weaken the sponsor’s negotiating position.
- Covenant flex: a right to add or tighten maintenance-style covenants is unusually sponsor-unfavorable and should be identified independently of ordinary pricing flex.
- Perfection mechanics: closing deliverables should be reviewed against any allowed post-closing cure period or delayed-perfection framework; blanket same-day perfection demands may be impractical.
- Indemnity and expense allocation: sponsor-side risk increases if indemnity or expense reimbursement is open-ended, uncapped, or missing customary conduct carve-outs.
- Clear market and syndication restrictions: any restriction on syndication communications or a post-closing tail should be assessed for breadth, duration, and operational effect on marketing.
- Use controlling authority only where the source package invokes a specific statute, regulation, rule, or defined market standard; otherwise frame conclusions as transactional convention rather than legal doctrine.

## 4. Analytical scaffolds
1. Map the deal timetable
   - Identify the outside date for commitment availability, the expected signing-to-closing path, and any marketing or syndication period.
   - Flag any date mismatch that could cause the financing to expire or become unstable before funding.
2. Test funding firmness
   - Isolate every condition to funding and classify it as customary, negotiable, or sponsor-unfriendly.
   - Separate target-specific conditions from broad market-outs, then assess the practical discretion each gives the lender.
3. Decompose flex
   - Review all flex provisions in one pass: price, OID, fee, maturity, collateral, prepayment, and covenant flex.
   - For each flex right, ask whether it is bounded by necessity for syndication or otherwise constrained; if not, flag the degree of lender discretion.
4. Reconcile conditions and deliverables
   - Compare the commitment letter’s closing conditions with the acquisition agreement summary and any ancillary materials.
   - Flag any condition that appears duplicative, overbroad, internally inconsistent, or not clearly tied to a customary closing deliverable.
5. Review collateral and post-closing mechanics
   - Determine which perfection steps must be completed at closing and which may be deferred.
   - If no workable post-closing cure period exists for burdensome deliverables, identify the operational risk.
6. Evaluate indemnity, expenses, and syndication controls
   - Check whether indemnity preserves customary carve-outs for wrongful conduct.
   - Check whether expenses are capped or otherwise bounded.
   - Check whether clear market restrictions or information controls are unusually broad in scope or duration.
7. Prioritize by transaction impact
   - Rank the most material issues first: commitment certainty, closing deliverability, syndication flexibility, then economic leakage.
   - Use a concise explanation for why the issue matters to the sponsor’s ability to close or preserve leverage.

## 5. Vertical / structural / temporal relationships
- Track how each issue changes across time: commitment signing, marketing, syndication, signing of the merger agreement, closing, and any post-closing cure period.
- Track how each issue propagates across documents: a clause in the commitment letter may be softened or worsened by the merger agreement summary, fee letter, or term sheet.
- Where multiple facilities, tranches, or financing sources exist, compare them vertically so that a problem in one piece is not masked by better language in another.
- If the package contains alternative formulations, note which version controls and whether the alternates create uncertainty.
- If the source documents use different defined terms for the same concept, reconcile them before drafting the issue.

## 6. Output structure conventions
- Draft a prioritized issues memo from the sponsor’s perspective; do not present a generic contract summary.
- Use a short executive summary first, focused on the issues most likely to affect closing certainty, syndicationability, and economics.
- Define a single ordinal severity scale at the top and apply it uniformly to every issue entry.
- For each issue, include: the provision or concept, the concern, the related document interaction, the downstream sponsor impact, a severity label, and a recommended resolution.
- Close each issue with a concrete sponsor-side consequence and an action-oriented fix; avoid issues that stop at description.
- If the source materials identify specific governing law, regulation, rule, or market authority relevant to a point, cite it by name and section in the issue discussion.
- End with a Recommended Actions block that assigns an imperative next step, the responsible role, and a timing anchor tied to the signing, marketing, or closing process.
- Use conventional memorandum headings rather than a rubric-shaped checklist.
