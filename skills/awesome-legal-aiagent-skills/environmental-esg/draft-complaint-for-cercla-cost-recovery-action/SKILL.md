# Skill: Draft Federal Court Complaint for CERCLA Cost Recovery Action

## 1. Subject-matter triage
- Treat the matter as a federal pleading assignment, not an issue list or memo.
- Identify all potentially responsible parties from the source set before drafting, and map each to the liability theory supported by the documents.
- Separate direct cost-recovery allegations from any contribution or allocation theory if both are available on the facts.
- Confirm whether the source documents support any parallel state spill-remediation claim and whether the same contamination and cost record can support it.

## 2. Failure modes the skill is correcting
- Drafts stay generic and do not tie each CERCLA element to site-specific facts for each defendant.
- Drafts allege contamination and cleanup in broad terms but omit the factual basis for response costs and their relation to the cleanup framework.
- Drafts blur owner/operator, arranger, and transporter theories instead of pleading the role and timeframe for each defendant distinctly.
- Drafts ignore successor-liability or corporate-continuity facts where the documents support them.
- Drafts omit parallel state-law recovery theories that may be available on the same record.
- Drafts state legal conclusions without naming the governing authority supporting them.
- Drafts fail to structure the complaint in conventional federal pleading form.

## 3. Legal frameworks / domain conventions that apply
- Plead CERCLA § 107(a) cost recovery by alleging a covered facility or release, a covered defendant, incurred response costs, and consistency with the applicable cleanup framework.
- Use the statutory categories for potentially responsible parties: current owner/operator, past owner/operator, arranger, and transporter.
- For arranger liability, plead facts showing an arrangement for disposal or treatment of hazardous substances, not merely a commercial sale.
- For transporter liability, plead facts showing the defendant selected the disposal or treatment site.
- For response costs, identify categories of costs, the relevant period, and the cleanup activity to which each relates.
- For cleanup consistency, plead the facts showing the response was authorized, tracked, documented, and implemented under the applicable requirements.
- If contribution is available, tie it to the statutory conditions that permit it and address allocation principles at a high level.
- If state spill or hazardous-substance law is available, plead it as an independent count with its own statutory basis and recovery theory.
- Cite controlling authority for each legal proposition relied on, including the statute, section, regulation, rule, or leading case as appropriate.

## 4. Analytical scaffolds
- First enumerate every defendant and every liability theory that the source documents plausibly support, then draft one factual sub-section per defendant.
- For each defendant, state the role, the time period, the hazardous substance facts, and the document source supporting that role.
- For each release or disposal pathway, identify the site media affected, the contamination pathway, and the investigation or remediation document that supports the allegation.
- For each response-cost category, state what was incurred, why it was necessary, and how the source documents show consistency with the applicable cleanup framework.
- For any arranger theory, identify the specific substance, the disposal or treatment arrangement, and the transactional or operational facts supporting intent to dispose.
- For any transporter theory, identify the transport activity, the destination site, and the facts showing site selection.
- For any successor-liability theory, plead the acquisition or continuity facts that connect the present defendant to the prior operator or owner.
- For each count, incorporate only the facts needed for that theory and then plead the statutory elements in element-by-element form.
- If the source set reflects prior settlement or governmental resolution, address its effect on remaining claims and any bar or offset issues before finalizing the counts.
- Verify pre-suit notice, administrative, or procedural prerequisites that the selected claims require before filing.

## 5. Vertical / structural / temporal relationships
- Plead site history chronologically: ownership and operations, release or discovery, regulatory attention, investigation, response actions, and continuing costs.
- When multiple defendants are involved, keep their roles and timeframes separate so the complaint does not collapse distinct conduct into one narrative.
- When costs span multiple phases, connect each phase to the corresponding response action and applicable legal basis.
- If future costs are sought, tie them to an ongoing or approved remedy and plead declaratory relief in support of that posture.
- If the cleanup record spans state and federal action, explain the relationship between the regimes without conflating their standards.

## 6. Output structure conventions
- Draft as a conventional federal complaint with caption, jurisdiction and venue, parties, factual background, causes of action, and prayer for relief.
- In the factual background, tell a chronological, document-supported story of the site, contamination, investigations, regulatory history, and response costs.
- Use count headings that identify the statutory or common-law basis and then incorporate prior allegations before pleading each element.
- For each count, anchor the allegations in the governing authority by name and section.
- Include a prayer for cost recovery, declaratory relief for future costs if supported, interest if available, and attorneys’ fees or other relief only where the governing law permits it.
- Keep the pleading ready for partner review and filing, with file-ready complaint language rather than a summary of what should be alleged.
- Ensure the output file is the operative complaint document requested by the task instructions.
