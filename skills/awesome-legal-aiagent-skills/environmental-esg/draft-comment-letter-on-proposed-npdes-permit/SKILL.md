# Skill: Draft Comment Letter on Proposed NPDES Permit

## 1. Subject-matter triage

- Treat the assignment as a formal regulatory comment submission, not a policy memo.
- Determine the permit status, receiving water, industrial category, and whether the record includes the current permit, fact sheet, monitoring data, TMDL materials, and any technical review.
- If only one proposed permit is at issue, state that plainly; if the record contains multiple permits, permit provisions, outfalls, waters, or pollutant groups, enumerate them before analysis and address each separately.
- Prioritize the operative permit terms first: effluent limits, monitoring, reporting, compliance schedules, special conditions, and fact sheet rationale.
- Draft the letter to oppose approval unless the record supports the permit under the governing Clean Water Act standards.

## 2. Failure modes the skill is correcting

- Drafts drift into generalized environmental advocacy instead of tying each objection to a specific permit deficiency and governing requirement.
- Drafts treat the letter as a narrative summary rather than a point-by-point legal submission grounded in the administrative record.
- Drafts omit comparison to the existing permit, causing missed anti-backsliding and antidegradation issues.
- Drafts ignore monitoring and reporting provisions even where the adequacy of limits depends on verification.
- Drafts make unsupported scientific assertions instead of using ambient data, receiving-water conditions, and technical review findings.
- Drafts state conclusions without naming the authority that supports them.
- Drafts fail to convert objections into concrete requested revisions, denial requests, or further review demands.
- Drafts bury the strongest issues; the letter should lead with the most legally significant and best-supported objections.

## 3. Legal frameworks / domain conventions that apply

- Clean Water Act NPDES permitting under 33 U.S.C. §§ 1311, 1312, 1313, 1314, and 1342, as implemented by EPA and delegated state permitting rules.
- Technology-based effluent limits and applicable effluent guidelines for the regulated industry category.
- Water-quality-based effluent limits where technology-based limits are insufficient to meet water quality standards.
- State water quality standards, including designated uses, numeric criteria, narrative criteria, and implementation procedures.
- Antidegradation requirements for maintaining existing uses and protecting higher-quality waters under the applicable state program.
- Anti-backsliding constraints on permit revisions that would relax limits or monitoring.
- TMDL consistency where the receiving water has an established load allocation or wasteload allocation.
- Mixing zone and dilution methodology only where the permit relies on them and the governing program authorizes them.
- Public participation requirements for notice, comment, and hearing requests under the applicable permit process.
- Administrative record review conventions: the fact sheet must explain the basis for each major term, and unsupported rationales are vulnerable.

## 4. Analytical scaffolds

- Start from the permit term, then test it against the controlling legal standard, then test the standard against the record.
- For each challenged term, use the same sequence:
  - identify the permit provision;
  - identify the legal source for the limit or condition;
  - compare the provision to the current permit, the fact sheet, and the supporting data;
  - explain why the term is insufficient, inconsistent, or inadequately justified;
  - state the specific revision requested.
- For each effluent limit:
  - determine whether it is technology-based, water-quality-based, or both;
  - verify that the limit is supported by the applicable guideline, model, calculation, or receiving-water analysis;
  - test whether the monitoring frequency and analytical method can actually demonstrate compliance.
- For any limit relaxation, apply an anti-backsliding comparison to the corresponding prior term and examine whether the record identifies a permitted exception.
- For any increase in loading or change in discharge configuration, examine whether antidegradation review was performed and whether the record supports the required protection analysis.
- For any receiving-water concern, connect ambient data, impairment status, designated uses, and pollutant behavior to the permit term at issue.
- For any TMDL issue, test consistency between the permit’s wasteload allocation and the applicable TMDL assumptions, endpoints, and implementation schedule.
- For each issue, close with:
  - the governing authority by name and section or part;
  - the specific factual deficiency in the permit record;
  - the practical consequence for water quality, compliance, or public participation;
  - the requested remedy, such as permit revision, additional analysis, or permit denial.
- Use an issue-by-issue structure rather than a generalized list of concerns.

## 5. Vertical / structural / temporal relationships

- Compare the proposed permit against the current permit term-by-term to identify relaxations, omissions, new conditions, and changed compliance pathways.
- Link the permit conditions to the fact sheet rationale; if the fact sheet does not justify a limit, condition, or monitoring design, flag the gap directly.
- Link receiving-water conditions upstream and downstream, when the record includes them, to show whether the proposed discharge will measurably affect the waterbody.
- Link effluent limits to associated monitoring and reporting obligations; a limit without a realistic verification mechanism is incomplete.
- Link special conditions to compliance deadlines and operational milestones so the letter can identify whether the schedule is realistic and lawful.
- Link the permit to any TMDL, watershed plan, or impairment listing in the administrative record to test whether the permit fits the broader regulatory framework.
- When multiple pollutants, outfalls, or receiving waters are implicated, keep the temporal and spatial relationships distinct; do not collapse them into one analysis.

## 6. Output structure conventions

- Write a formal public comment letter with standard letterhead elements, date, recipient, permit identifier, and a clear subject line.
- Open with a concise summary of opposition and the principal legal and technical defects.
- Use a defined severity scale at the top if the letter discusses multiple issues, and apply it consistently to each issue discussed.
- Organize the body as numbered issues, each with:
  - a short issue heading;
  - the governing authority;
  - the permit deficiency;
  - the supporting record evidence;
  - the consequence if uncorrected;
  - the requested action.
- Keep the tone formal, record-based, and exacting; avoid rhetorical excess.
- End with a distinct recommendations section that states the requested actions in imperative form, identifies the responsible permitting authority or applicant-facing reviewer, and ties each request to the comment deadline or permit decision milestone.
- If warranted by the record, include a request for a public hearing and explain why oral testimony or an expanded record is necessary.
- Conclude with a clear statement that the commenter opposes issuance of the permit as proposed unless the identified defects are corrected.
