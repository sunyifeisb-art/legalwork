# Skill: Analyze Counterparty Markup of Administrative Settlement Agreement — Redline Analysis Memorandum

## 1. Subject-matter triage
- Treat the marked-up administrative settlement as a negotiated public-law instrument, not a generic commercial contract.
- Identify at the outset whether the markup affects liability allocation, covenant scope, performance obligations, compliance mechanics, penalty architecture, or remedial timing.
- If multiple parties, properties, operable units, work phases, or related instruments are in play, enumerate them before analysis and keep the treatment separate for each.
- Read the redline against the draft and supporting documents as a single record; do not evaluate a clause in isolation from the settlement framework it implements.

## 2. Failure modes the skill is correcting
- Treating redlines as isolated drafting edits without mapping each change to statutory, regulatory, or policy consequences.
- Missing how a small textual edit can shift settlement scope, contribution protection, reopener risk, or enforcement leverage.
- Failing to connect the markup to cost estimates, allocation summaries, reporting obligations, or other supporting materials that control the practical effect.
- Accepting overbroad liability limitation, force majeure, or cure language that is inconsistent with administrative settlement practice.
- Describing issues without stating severity, source-doc support, and a concrete negotiating response.
- Writing conclusions without naming the controlling legal or policy authority for the proposition relied on.

## 3. Legal frameworks / domain conventions that apply
- Environmental liability framework: identify the liable parties, the allocation structure, and how the markup shifts exposure among settling and non-settling parties.
- Administrative settlement authority: assess the scope of the settlement, the basis for covenant relief, and any reservation-of-rights or reopener language.
- Contribution protection: confirm that protection tracks the settlement scope and is conditioned on performance and compliance milestones.
- Cleanup-program compliance requirements: compare proposed work, reporting, documentation, and cost-recovery mechanics against applicable cleanup-program standards and reasonableness expectations.
- Financial assurance: evaluate whether the proposed mechanism satisfies the relevant agency criteria for strength, liquidity, and enforceability.
- Stipulated penalties: assess whether any rates, triggers, grace periods, or caps preserve meaningful compliance incentives and enforceability.
- Force majeure and excuse provisions: compare proposed triggers against settlement-appropriate categories and exclude purely commercial hardship unless the agreement clearly permits it.
- Use the controlling authority that supports each legal proposition, whether statutory, regulatory, or policy-based, and name it explicitly in the analysis.

## 4. Analytical scaffolds
- Work through the redline in section order and analyze every substantive change, even if the edit appears minor.
- For each issue, state: the provision affected, what changed, the controlling authority or policy basis, the client impact, and the recommended negotiating posture.
- Tie every material concern to the relevant source documents, including any cost estimate, allocation summary, technical report, compliance schedule, or governance document that the markup affects.
- For allocation or exposure changes, describe the direction of shift and the affected pool or phase; do not reduce the analysis to generic “more risk” language.
- For covenant, release, or contribution-protection edits, test whether the scope, conditions, and carveouts align with the settlement’s actual remedial footprint.
- For reopener or reservation-of-rights changes, test whether any deletion or narrowing is consistent with mandatory agency practice and enforcement flexibility.
- For financial assurance edits, test the sufficiency of the proposed form, timing, and enforcement mechanics against the governing requirements.
- For timeline edits, trace cascading effects across downstream milestones, approvals, reporting dates, and completion obligations.
- For penalty edits, assess whether the structure creates a genuine compliance incentive and preserves practical enforcement leverage.
- Close each issue with three elements: the scale or threshold implicated by the source materials, the related clause or document interaction, and the downstream consequence for the client.
- If the source materials contain a single relevant party, period, or scenario, state that expressly; otherwise analyze each separately rather than collapsing them.

## 5. Vertical / structural / temporal relationships
- Map how changes affect the relative obligations of each settling and non-settling party, including contribution exposure and orphan-share risk.
- Track whether one party’s revised language shifts residual liability or performance burden to another party.
- Trace sequential milestone changes through later reporting, access, remedy, approval, or certification obligations.
- Compare the markup against any parallel or prior consent decree, administrative order, side agreement, or related settlement so conflicts are visible.
- Where the redline alters a defined term, apply the change consistently through every downstream use of that term.

## 6. Output structure conventions
- Produce a formal redline analysis memorandum, not a generic comment list.
- Use conventional sections: Executive Summary, Section-by-Section Analysis, Consolidated Issues Table, and Recommendations.
- Executive Summary: give the overall risk profile, the mix of critical versus significant versus minor issues, and the main negotiating priorities.
- Section-by-Section Analysis: for each redlined provision, state the markup, the legal or policy concern, the client impact, and the recommended response.
- Consolidated Issues Table: include one row per issue with provision reference, issue category, severity, and recommended action.
- Use an ordinal severity scale defined once and apply it uniformly, such as Critical / Significant / Minor.
- In every entry, include a severity label, a short rationale, the governing authority, the document interaction, and the client consequence.
- End with a Recommended Actions block that assigns an imperative action, the responsible role, and a timing anchor tied to the transaction or regulatory milestone.
- When drafting any markup language for the analysis, make every substantive edit identifiable in plain text as well as by formatting, using explicit textual change markers and a brief rationale.
- Keep the file name exactly as instructed: redline-analysis-memorandum.docx.
