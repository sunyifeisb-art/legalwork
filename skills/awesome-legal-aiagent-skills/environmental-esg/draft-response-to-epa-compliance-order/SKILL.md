# Skill: Draft Response to EPA RCRA Compliance Order

## 1. Subject-matter triage

- Confirm the order is an administrative enforcement response under RCRA and identify the operative statutory, regulatory, and order provisions before drafting.
- Separate conceded violations from contested allegations, and separate merits defenses from mitigation-only arguments.
- Determine whether the response should include factual admissions, proposed corrective actions, a settlement posture, and a request for penalty adjustment or payment terms.

## 2. Failure modes the skill is correcting

- Baseline drafts a global narrative response without organizing the analysis count-by-count to address each alleged violation separately.
- Baseline omits defenses commonly available in enforcement responses, such as emergency response, force majeure, or good-faith compliance, that are supported by facility records.
- Baseline does not use available environmental, health, and safety records, compliance history, and inspection documents to substantiate factual defenses for each contested count.
- Baseline proposes a compliance schedule without assessing technical feasibility of each milestone based on available operational and construction data.
- Baseline states legal conclusions without tying them to the controlling RCRA provision, implementing regulation, or penalty authority.
- Baseline discusses alleged violations without distinguishing the inspection period from post-inspection corrective action and resulting mitigation.

## 3. Legal frameworks / domain conventions that apply

- Identify the order authority, the alleged RCRA obligations, and the penalty or settlement framework implicated by the agency action; cite the controlling statute, regulation, or order provision for each legal proposition.
- Common RCRA compliance topics include generator status, accumulation limits and timing, container management, labeling, inspection, manifesting, land disposal restrictions, and recordkeeping.
- Penalty analysis typically turns on gravity, economic benefit, history of noncompliance, good-faith efforts, ability to pay, and timely corrective action; use the applicable agency penalty policy or comparable enforcement factors.
- Defenses and mitigation themes commonly include factual nonoccurrence, ambiguity in the cited requirement, prompt correction, emergency conditions, force majeure, and lack of economic benefit; support each with the governing rule or policy.
- If corrective action, reporting, or ongoing operational controls are implicated, align any proposed schedule with the applicable regulatory process and permit or order obligations.
- Use only authority-supported defenses; do not overstate equitable arguments where the cited rule requires a specific notice, documentation, or timing showing.

## 4. Analytical scaffolds

- Enumerate each count first, then analyze each count in the same sequence as the order.
- For each count, identify:
  - the cited regulatory requirement;
  - the alleged factual condition;
  - the relevant time period;
  - whether the allegation is denied, admitted, or partially admitted;
  - the records that support the response.
- For each contested count, test the agency’s theory against facility records, manifests, inspection logs, training records, photographs, correspondence, and corrective-action documents.
- For each admitted or partially admitted count, state the corrective action already taken, the remaining work, and the legal or operational reason the violation should be treated as mitigated.
- For force majeure or emergency-based defenses, identify the triggering event, the duration, the specific causal chain to the alleged violation, and the documentation and notice showing compliance with the applicable requirement.
- For penalty reduction, address each relevant factor separately and connect the facts to the requested adjustment.
- For compliance scheduling, break the work into milestones that are technically realistic, sequenced, and tied to actual procurement, construction, shutdown, testing, training, or certification needs.
- Draft the response as a formal administrative letter that includes count-by-count responses, penalty mitigation, a proposed schedule, and a settlement posture or reservation of rights as appropriate.

## 5. Vertical / structural / temporal relationships

- Compare the inspection date, alleged violation period, and response date to show whether the cited condition existed when the agency says it did.
- Distinguish pre-order conditions from post-inspection corrective action; later remediation may mitigate penalty even if it does not erase a past violation.
- If multiple sites, waste streams, or operating units are involved, keep each one separate so that facts, defenses, and corrective actions do not bleed across counts.
- If a penalty component is said to reflect delayed compliance, address the timing of the alleged delay and any intervening efforts that reduced or eliminated environmental risk.
- If the order references ongoing obligations, ensure the proposed schedule does not conflict with deadlines already imposed by law, permit, consent process, or prior agency correspondence.

## 6. Output structure conventions

- Write the deliverable as a formal business letter or administrative response, with date, recipient, subject line, order reference, and clear closing request.
- Use a count-by-count body in the same order as the order; for each count, include the allegation, response, applicable authority, supporting facts, and current status.
- Include a separate penalty section that requests reduction, modification, or deferral using the recognized penalty factors and supporting facts.
- Include a compliance schedule section with a milestone table or list that gives specific tasks, sequencing, and target dates.
- Include a settlement or resolution paragraph that preserves appropriate rights while inviting resolution on defined terms.
- Keep the tone professional, factual, and regulatory; avoid advocacy language that is unsupported by the record.
- If the source materials identify a required deadline or response window, state compliance with it expressly.
- Deliver the primary file named `compliance-order-response-letter.docx` and ensure it contains the operative response letter, not merely an outline or summary.
