# Skill: Identify Issues in Zoning Compliance Report for Mixed-Use Acquisition Site

## 1. Subject-matter triage
- Treat the zoning compliance report as a secondary synthesis, not the source of truth.
- Identify the governing ordinance version, the survey-based site facts, the variance or other land-use approval terms, the environmental restrictions, and the purchase agreement zoning condition before assessing compliance.
- If multiple site components, approvals, or use scenarios are implicated, enumerate them first and analyze each separately rather than collapsing them into a single pass.
- Separate current-use compliance from proposed-use feasibility; mixed-use acquisition questions often turn on both.

## 2. Failure modes the skill is correcting
- Accepting the report’s conclusions without independently checking them against the ordinance text actually in force.
- Missing discrepancies between the report’s stated site dimensions, setbacks, parking, use mix, or improvement locations and the survey.
- Overlooking variance or approval conditions that narrow, supplement, or limit otherwise permissive ordinance language.
- Failing to test whether nonconforming status, conditional approval conditions, or reconstruction limits constrain the property after closing.
- Ignoring conflicts between environmental restrictions and zoning assumptions for redevelopment or reuse.
- Missing that an identified zoning defect may also trigger a closing-condition failure under the purchase agreement.
- Treating descriptive gaps as harmless even where they leave the buyer exposed to operational, regulatory, or transactional risk.

## 3. Legal frameworks / domain conventions that apply
- Zoning compliance reports are only as reliable as the ordinance text, survey data, and approval record on which they rely.
- Ordinance analysis should track the operative standards for permitted use, dimensional controls, parking, signage, special or conditional uses, and any express nonconforming-use rules.
- Variance or approval letters must be read as condition-bearing land-use permissions; all stated conditions are part of the compliance analysis.
- Survey evidence controls the factual premises for setback, lot area, frontage, encroachment, improvement placement, and similar dimensional conclusions.
- Nonconforming use and structure doctrines typically preserve only the extent of the existing lawful condition and often limit expansion, restoration, or change of use under the governing ordinance.
- Environmental summaries may identify controls, notices, or institutional constraints that can restrict development assumptions even if zoning technically permits the use.
- Purchase agreement closing conditions should be checked for any seller deliverable tied to zoning compliance, accuracy of representations, or absence of specified land-use defects.
- For any legal proposition stated in the memo, identify the governing authority by name and section, ordinance provision, approval condition, or contractual clause as applicable.

## 4. Analytical scaffolds
- Read the report’s conclusion section first and list each claimed point of compliance or non-issue.
- For each claim, verify the cited ordinance provision, the relevant survey fact, and any approval condition that qualifies the conclusion.
- Compare the report’s dimensional and use conclusions against the survey and approval documents; where the documents disagree, treat the discrepancy itself as an issue.
- Check whether the report addresses all conditions, exceptions, and temporal limits attached to any variance, special permit, or similar approval.
- Assess whether any environmental restriction would impair the existing use, planned improvements, or the assumed zoning pathway.
- Check the purchase agreement excerpts to determine whether the identified issue affects a zoning-related closing condition, representation, or covenant.
- For each issue, close the analysis with: the scale of the problem as reflected in the source materials, the interacting source document or clause, and the consequence for the buyer.
- Assign a severity level to every issue using a uniform ordinal scale defined at the outset, and keep the scale consistent throughout.

## 5. Vertical / structural / temporal relationships
- More specific approval terms control over general ordinance permissions for the same subject matter.
- Survey facts control the physical premises underlying setback, coverage, easement, access, and location analyses.
- Temporal sequencing matters: later ordinance amendments, later approval conditions, or later environmental restrictions may change the compliance picture.
- A land-use restriction that is legally compatible in the abstract may still block the buyer’s intended mixed-use plan once all approvals and environmental constraints are read together.

## 6. Output structure conventions
- Produce an issues memorandum organized first by severity tier and then by zoning topic within each tier.
- Define the severity scale once at the top, then use it uniformly for every entry.
- For each issue, include: source document and section reference, the issue statement, the controlling ordinance or approval language, the comparison to the survey or other source document, the severity rating, and the downstream consequence.
- End with a concise executive summary that identifies the issues by severity and flags any issue that may fail a zoning-related closing condition.
- End with a Recommended Actions block that states each action in the imperative, names the responsible role, and gives a timing anchor tied to the transaction or regulatory timeline.
- Use industry-conventional memo headings and do not mirror any hidden checklist or rubric wording.
- Deliverable filename must match the task instructions exactly.
