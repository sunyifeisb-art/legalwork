# Skill: Identify Issues in Proposed Environmental Consent Decree — Board-Level Issue Memorandum

## 1. Subject-matter triage
- Treat the decree, permits, technical exhibits, and compliance materials as one integrated record.
- Build the issue list around the facility, the regulated media, and the operative deadlines; do not analyze isolated clauses without the related permit term, schedule item, or exhibit.
- If the record contains multiple regulated parameters, deadlines, or obligation buckets, enumerate them first and analyze each separately.
- Keep the memo board-facing: elevate legal and operational risk, not drafting trivia.

## 2. Failure modes the skill is correcting
- Decree limits may be stricter than permit limits, creating independent compliance exposure even where the permit is satisfied.
- Broad agency interpretive language may let the government control compliance determinations without an objective review standard.
- Stipulated penalties may be flat, cumulative, or otherwise out of proportion to the violation type.
- Insurance analysis is often omitted even though penalties, response costs, defense costs, and third-party claims may be treated differently under the policy stack.
- Compliance deadlines may be technically possible in the abstract but unrealistic when sequencing, procurement, shutdown windows, or testing dependencies are considered.
- Injunctive relief may sweep more broadly than the conduct, facility, or regulatory issue requires.
- A useful issue memo does not stop at description; it ties each issue to scale, related provisions, and business consequence.

## 3. Legal frameworks / domain conventions that apply
- **Consent decree vs. permit compliance:** Compare each decree obligation against the corresponding permit requirement and any incorporated appendix or exhibit. A decree can operate as an independent source of obligation. Cite the decree provision, the matched permit term, and any controlling environmental authority referenced in the record.
- **Agency discretion and review standard:** If the decree grants the agency unilateral interpretive or compliance-determination power, check whether the text cabins that power with an objective standard, good-faith standard, reasonableness standard, or defined dispute path. Identify the controlling provision in the decree and any incorporated dispute-resolution clause.
- **Stipulated penalties:** Review whether penalties are triggered per day, per event, per parameter, or per facility condition; compare the trigger structure to the seriousness and duration of the underlying violation and any cure period or grace mechanism.
- **Insurance coverage:** Analyze whether likely exposure falls within, or outside, coverage for civil penalties, cleanup or remedial costs, defense costs, third-party bodily injury or property damage, and any pollution, intentional acts, contractual liability, or sublimit exclusions.
- **Compliance schedule feasibility:** Measure each deadline against the operational path the decree itself contemplates, including engineering, procurement, installation, commissioning, testing, reporting, and certification steps.
- **Scope of injunctive relief:** Ask whether the relief is tailored to the facility and conduct at issue, or whether it extends to unrelated assets, future conditions, or open-ended obligations without clear termination criteria.
- **Authority citation discipline:** When stating a legal proposition, identify the governing authority named in the materials or the standard authority commonly used for that proposition; do not present an unsupported conclusion.

## 4. Analytical scaffolds
1. **Limit-by-limit comparison**
   - For each regulated parameter, compare decree text, permit text, and any technical appendix.
   - Identify whether the decree adds a tighter numeric limit, a lower threshold, a shorter averaging period, a new sampling method, or a more onerous reporting obligation.
2. **Independent exposure mapping**
   - State whether a decree breach creates exposure even if the permit is met.
   - Cross-reference any savings clause, incorporation clause, or survival provision that affects that answer.
3. **Interpretive control check**
   - Locate every clause giving the agency authority to interpret, approve, certify, or reject compliance submissions.
   - Flag any absence of a review standard, escalation path, or objective benchmark.
4. **Penalty calibration review**
   - Match each penalty trigger to the affected obligation and assess whether the rate escalates rationally with duration, seriousness, and administrative burden.
   - Flag stacking, duplicative triggers, or penalties that appear detached from the underlying risk.
5. **Coverage gap scan**
   - Separate exposure into penalties, remediation, defense, and third-party claims.
   - Identify any exclusion, sublimit, consent-to-settle condition, notice requirement, or pollution carve-out that weakens the coverage position.
6. **Schedule realism test**
   - Test whether the decree’s dates allow enough time for design, procurement, construction, validation, and operational stabilization.
   - Flag deadline clusters or sequences that assume immediate execution despite known dependencies.
7. **Tailoring and scope test**
   - Compare the relief requested to the stated violation, source area, and regulated media.
   - Flag mandates that reach beyond the relevant operations or continue without a clear end point.

## 5. Vertical / structural / temporal relationships
- Always analyze the decree against the permit and any incorporated attachments together, not separately.
- Track how a later deadline depends on an earlier certification, study, or agency approval.
- If one obligation gates another, identify that dependency and the risk of cascading noncompliance.
- If the materials include multiple facilities, units, or operating modes, separate them rather than assuming one rule fits all.
- Distinguish present obligations from monitoring, maintenance, and long-tail reporting duties that survive initial implementation.

## 6. Output structure conventions
- Deliver a board-level issue memorandum with a short executive framing, a severity legend using a uniform ordinal scale, and numbered issues in priority order.
- For each issue, include:
  - the **severity** level;
  - the relevant decree provision and any interacting permit or exhibit provision;
  - the specific concern;
  - a comparison or scale reference drawn from the source materials;
  - the downstream legal, operational, financial, or regulatory consequence;
  - a recommended negotiating or review step.
- Use conventional memo headings rather than a rubric-like checklist.
- Include a side-by-side comparison table for decree limits and existing permit limits for each regulated parameter.
- End with a **Recommended Actions** block that assigns an imperative action, the responsible role, and a timing anchor tied to the next filing, response, meeting, or compliance milestone.
- Keep recommendations practical and process-oriented; frame them as next steps for counsel, the business owner, or the relevant officer.
