# Skill: Identify Inconsistencies and Vulnerabilities in Prior Witness Testimony — Impeachment Memorandum

## 1. Subject-matter triage

- Treat the assignment as a cross-source impeachment review, not a general chronology summary.
- First inventory every statement-bearing forum in the source set: sworn testimony, investigative interviews, proffer or cooperation sessions, internal interviews, declarations, and written witness communications.
- Separate witness statements from document facts; use documents to test and contextualize testimony, not to merge the two unless the witness adopted the document content.
- If only one forum exists, state that affirmatively and explain why a true inconsistency analysis is unavailable; then focus on internal vulnerability, omission, or qualification issues within that forum.

## 2. Failure modes the skill is correcting

- The analysis stops at “inconsistency” without identifying which statement is more likely false, which forum carries exposure, and which legal framework governs that forum.
- Material inconsistencies are missed because the review does not compare the witness against documents and other witnesses, especially where the witness minimizes a meeting, communication, or decision in one account.
- Timing is ignored; an earlier informal or non-sworn account followed by later sworn testimony may signal revision rather than innocent memory drift.
- The memo fails to test whether the inconsistency undermines a defense element, especially good-faith reliance on outside professionals or any other affirmative defense theory reflected in the materials.
- Statements are described generally without precise source attribution, making impeachment use difficult.
- Issues are listed without prioritization, severity, or follow-up recommendations.

## 3. Legal frameworks / domain conventions that apply

- Identify the governing false-statement framework for each forum before evaluating exposure. Sworn testimony, unsworn but protected or restricted sessions, and informal interviews do not share the same consequences.
- For sworn statements, assess perjury or equivalent false-testimony exposure under the applicable jurisdictional rule.
- For non-sworn or protected sessions, assess exposure under the governing false-statement, proffer, cooperation, or agreement-based framework reflected in the source materials or standard practice.
- When a statement is materially inconsistent across forums, assess which account is more likely false and whether the discrepancy is deliberate, reckless, or plausibly explained by context, ambiguity, or faulty recollection.
- Use ordinary impeachment conventions: direct contradiction, omission inconsistent with later detail, qualification that narrows a prior broad statement, and minimization of a meeting or role later shown to be substantive.
- Where the matter implicates reliance on outside professionals, test whether the witness’s later account supports or erodes the elements of good-faith reliance on advice of counsel, auditors, accountants, or other specialists.
- Do not treat every discrepancy as equal; legal significance depends on materiality, forum, and whether the point bears on intent, knowledge, reliance, or core event sequence.

## 4. Analytical scaffolds

1. **Forum inventory**
   - List each forum in chronological order.
   - For each, state date, setting, whether sworn or unsworn, and the governing exposure framework.
   - Identify whether the forum was earlier or later than the others and whether timing makes revision risk more likely.

2. **Issue-by-issue comparison**
   - For each inconsistency, quote or closely paraphrase the competing statements with precise source attribution.
   - Anchor each statement to date, page/line, exhibit, or document reference.
   - Compare the statements side by side and state the factual point in dispute.

3. **Characterization**
   - Classify each inconsistency as minor, significant, or critical.
   - Use a uniform ordinal severity scale defined once at the outset.
   - Base the classification on materiality, centrality to the case, clarity of contradiction, and availability of an innocent explanation.

4. **False-statement and impeachment exposure**
   - Identify the forum in which the false-statement issue most likely arises.
   - State which statement is more likely false and why.
   - Distinguish between impeachment value and affirmative exposure; a statement can be useful for cross-examination even if it does not support a standalone charge or claim.

5. **Defense-impact analysis**
   - Test each inconsistency against the elements of any reliance, knowledge, intent, or good-faith theory implicated by the record.
   - Explain whether the inconsistency merely weakens credibility or materially undermines a defense theme.

6. **Document and cross-witness corroboration**
   - Compare the witness’s account with documents and with other witnesses on the same event.
   - Flag where a non-party document or another witness likely contradicts the subject witness on a material point.
   - Treat corroborating conflict as more serious when the witness downplays a meeting, approval, warning, or instruction that another source shows was substantive.

7. **Temporal significance**
   - Pay close attention to sequence.
   - Earlier statements can constrain later testimony; later testimony that departs from earlier accounts may suggest strategic revision.
   - Identify whether the inconsistency becomes more or less serious because of when each statement was made.

8. **Priority sorting**
   - Rank the strongest impeachment points first.
   - Prioritize contradictions that go to knowledge, intent, reliance, or core event mechanics over peripheral recollections.
   - Include only issues that can be tied to source material with usable specificity.

## 5. Vertical / structural / temporal relationships

- Read the record vertically: use testimony, exhibits, and related communications to reconstruct the same event across time and across forums.
- Read the record horizontally: compare the subject witness against other witnesses on the same date, meeting, or transaction step.
- When a witness calls a meeting or conversation informal, administrative, or insignificant, test that characterization against documents and corroborating witnesses.
- When later testimony fills in detail omitted earlier, assess whether the added detail is consistent with the earlier account or appears to repair a prior gap.
- Treat any sequence where an earlier non-sworn account is followed by a more polished sworn account as especially relevant to credibility and possible false-statement exposure.

## 6. Output structure conventions

- Deliver a defense impeachment vulnerability memorandum organized by inconsistency, not as a narrative summary.
- Start with a short priority section identifying the most consequential inconsistencies and why they matter.
- State the severity scale once, then apply it consistently to every entry.
- For each inconsistency, include:
  - side-by-side source comparison;
  - precise source attribution;
  - severity;
  - false-statement or impeachment exposure;
  - defense impact, including any effect on reliance or other substantive defenses;
  - any corroborating or conflicting document or witness source;
  - recommended follow-up or cross-examination angle.
- Use conventional legal memo headings and concise bullets; avoid prose that repeats the same point without adding source-backed analysis.
- End with a Recommended Actions section that directs next steps by role and urgency, such as targeted follow-up, document collection, deposition prep, or witness outline refinement.
