# Skill: Assess Settlement Value Range for Product Liability Crush Injury Case — Litigation Settlement Memorandum

## 1. Subject-matter triage

- Treat this as a pre-mediation valuation exercise, not a merits brief.
- Identify the governing jurisdiction, causes of action, injury mechanism, claimed losses, insurance stack, and any settlement constraints before pricing the case.
- If the file includes more than one plaintiff, defendant, policy layer, injury episode, or valuation scenario, enumerate them explicitly and assess each separately before synthesizing a range.
- If only one claimant and one primary liability event are in scope, state that affirmatively and avoid blending distinct exposures into one average case.

## 2. Failure modes the skill is correcting

- Anchoring to a single damages figure from one expert without reconciling competing methodologies or the reasons the opinions diverge.
- Ignoring how liability strength changes the weight assigned to plaintiff-side damages, defense-side damages, and verdict risk.
- Omitting the insurance and indemnity structure, which can cap practical settlement value and alter opening and reserve posture.
- Treating comparable verdicts or settlements as directly portable without adjusting for venue, injury severity, causation disputes, time, and litigation posture.
- Presenting one number instead of a bounded range with a defensible floor, target, and ceiling tied to identified risk assumptions.
- Failing to connect valuation judgments to controlling legal standards for product liability, damages, admissibility, and recoverability.
- Offering conclusions without a strategy for the first demand or offer, the negotiation path, and the next decision point.

## 3. Legal frameworks / domain conventions that apply

- Product liability theories commonly require separate analysis of design defect, manufacturing defect, and failure-to-warn, because each theory changes proof burdens and settlement leverage.
- Personal-injury damages generally separate economic losses from non-economic harm; if punitive damages are legally available, assess them only under the governing substantive standard and any applicable cap.
- Causation and comparative fault doctrines materially affect valuation because they can reduce both liability probability and damages recoverability.
- Expert admissibility governs how much settlement weight to assign to competing damages opinions; under Daubert v. Merrell Dow Pharmaceuticals, Inc. and Federal Rule of Evidence 702, methodology weaknesses are valuation leverage.
- Comparable outcomes are market evidence, not mechanical precedent; they require adjustment for venue, timing, severity, liability posture, and insurance context.
- Insurance coverage analysis should include primary and excess layers, any reservation of rights, and any coverage disputes that may affect net recovery.
- Settlement communications may be protected by applicable mediation confidentiality rules or agreement-based protections; reflect the governing rule without over-disclosing negotiation positions.
- The memo should be framed as an advisory valuation product: a reasoned estimate, not a final adjudication.

## 4. Analytical scaffolds

- Start with the pleadings, demand materials, and key factual chronology to isolate the injury mechanism, product condition, alleged defect theory, and claimed loss categories.
- Build a liability matrix:
  - identify each asserted theory;
  - evaluate proof on defect, notice, warning adequacy, causation, and defense themes;
  - note any comparative-fault or misuse arguments;
  - translate liability strength into settlement weight.
- Build a damages matrix:
  - separate past and future economic loss;
  - separate pain and suffering, disfigurement, impairment, and loss of enjoyment where supported;
  - consider punitive exposure only if the governing law and facts plausibly support it.
- Reconcile expert opinions:
  - identify what each expert measured;
  - note the data inputs, assumptions, and methodological disputes;
  - explain which assumptions are more settlement-relevant and why.
- Review comparable outcomes:
  - use verdicts and settlements as calibration points;
  - adjust for venue, procedural posture, case age, severity, and liability strength;
  - avoid using a raw headline figure without contextual adjustment.
- Assess insurance and collectability:
  - identify available limits and layers;
  - note exclusions, defenses, reservations, and any likely coverage friction;
  - distinguish gross exposure from practical settlement ceiling.
- Convert the analysis into scenario bands:
  - low-end outcome if liability weakens and damages proof compresses;
  - central range if both sides retain meaningful risk;
  - high-end outcome if liability and damages proof strengthen and policy limits permit.
- Derive an opening offer or demand strategy that is intentionally distant from target value but still credible, and explain the concession path that should follow mediation feedback.
- Tie every valuation conclusion to a stated assumption set so the reader can see what would move the range up or down.

## 5. Vertical / structural / temporal relationships

- Liability findings should drive damages weighting; weaker causation or defect proof lowers the share of the range assigned to plaintiff expert figures.
- Coverage limits set a practical ceiling unless there is a documented basis for personal exposure or additional collectible assets.
- Older comparable outcomes should be time-adjusted and interpreted for shifts in jury behavior, venue trends, and inflation.
- Future medical or wage components should be discounted or probability-weighted where the source materials support that treatment.
- The opening offer strategy should track the same liability and damages assumptions used to build the settlement range, so the negotiation posture is internally consistent.

## 6. Output structure conventions

- Write a conventional legal memorandum with clear headings for issue, brief answer, liability assessment, damages assessment, expert reconciliation, comparable outcomes, insurance/collectability, recommended range, and negotiation strategy.
- Present the valuation as a bounded range with a low, target, and high anchor, each linked to a specific factual or legal assumption set.
- State the principal assumptions and valuation drivers up front so the recipient can see what the range depends on.
- Include a separate opening offer strategy section that identifies the first move, the intended signaling effect, and the next concession checkpoint.
- End with a concise Recommended Actions block that assigns next steps to the responsible lawyer or team role and ties each step to the mediation timeline.
- Keep the memo defensible and operational: no narrative excess, no standalone conclusion unsupported by the cited facts and governing law, and no unsupported legal assertion without naming the controlling authority.
