# Skill: Draft Discovery Responses and Objections to RFAs and RFPs in Breach of Supply Agreement Litigation

## 1. Subject-matter triage
- Identify the governing discovery set, the request numbering, the response deadline, and any stipulation or court order that changes timing, format, or privilege-log requirements.
- Separate requests for admission from requests for production before drafting; each has a different response posture and different risk if handled reflexively.
- Review the pleadings, the supply agreement, and any precedent responses before deciding what can safely be admitted, denied, qualified, or objected to.
- If the source set includes multiple requests, enumerate them first and analyze each one individually; do not use a generic response strategy across dissimilar requests.
- Preserve the possibility that a single request may require a mixed response: objection as to part, admission or denial as to the rest, or production subject to limitations.

## 2. Failure modes the skill is correcting
- Denying an RFA that concerns an undisputed fact, which can create needless expense exposure under Rule 36 of the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure or the applicable state analogue.
- Failing to distinguish between a request for a fact, a request for a legal conclusion, and a compound request that bundles several propositions together.
- Answering an RFP with objections only, without saying whether responsive materials will be produced, withheld, or produced in part.
- Using boilerplate objections that do not track the actual request, the actual burden, or the actual privilege issue.
- Missing the litigation rationale behind each response, so the formal response and the strategy memo do not support each other.
- Forgetting that admissions can conclusively establish a matter, making careless admissions harder to unwind later.
- Omitting the controlling authority for the objection or response position, which weakens the memo and makes the response look conclusory.

## 3. Legal frameworks / domain conventions that apply
- For RFAs, apply Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 36 or the governing state rule: after reasonable inquiry, the responding party must admit, deny, or state that it cannot truthfully admit or deny, and must fairly respond to the substance of the request.
- For RFAs, a denial should not be evasive; if only part of a request is supportable, qualify the response and admit the undisputed portion.
- For RFAs, a request that seeks a legal conclusion may be objectionable, but the objection should be tied to the governing rule and the request should still be answered to the extent it seeks facts.
- For RFAs, a compound request may be objected to as compound, ambiguous, or confusing, while still responding to any separable factual component.
- For RFPs, apply Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 34 or the governing state rule: state whether responsive documents or ESI will be produced, identify any scope limits, and say if materials are withheld on the basis of an objection.
- For RFPs, if materials are withheld, the response should preserve the objection and describe the nature of the withheld material with enough specificity to be meaningful.
- For privilege issues, use the attorney-client privilege, work-product doctrine, or any applicable confidentiality protection, and reference a privilege log or similar log where required.
- For all legal propositions stated in the memo or the formal responses, cite the controlling rule, statute, regulation, or doctrine by name rather than relying on conclusory phrasing.

## 4. Analytical scaffolds
- Start with the request text, then ask: what exactly is being sought, what is undisputed, what is disputed, what is privileged, and what is burdensome or overbroad.
- For each RFA, decide whether to admit, deny, qualify, object, or state inability to admit or deny after reasonable inquiry.
- Favor admissions on noncontroversial facts that help narrow the case and do not concede the disputed breach, causation, damages, or defenses issues.
- For each RFA denial, ensure the denial fairly meets the substance of the request and does not leave an implied admission.
- For each RFP, decide whether the objection is total or partial, whether production will occur subject to the objection, and whether a privilege log or confidentiality designation is needed.
- If a request is overbroad or ambiguous, identify the defect specifically and tie it to the request’s wording, scope, or time period.
- Use precedent responses only as a formatting and phrasing aid; adapt them to the present requests and facts rather than copying them mechanically.
- Draft the strategy memo as the explanation layer for the responses: why each request is answered the way it is, what risk the position avoids, and what factual or legal issue remains preserved.

## 5. Vertical / structural / temporal relationships
- Track the relationship between the request, the pleadings, the supply agreement, and any scheduling order; a response that is technically plausible can still be wrong if it ignores a deadline, a defined term, or a governing limitation.
- If the request turns on whether a fact is pre-breach, post-breach, or tied to performance under a specific contractual period, anchor the response to that temporal frame.
- If a request reaches across multiple periods, products, shipments, notices, or performance events, analyze each discrete slice separately before deciding whether a single response can safely cover them.
- Where a request intersects with privilege, confidentiality, or trade-secret concerns, state the limitation in the response and align it with any protective-order procedure in the source materials.
- Keep the memo and the formal responses synchronized: the memo should explain the same positions that appear in the written responses, not a different strategy.

## 6. Output structure conventions
- Produce two distinct deliverables: a discovery-response-plan memo and a separate set of formal RFA/RFP responses.
- The discovery-response-plan memo should be organized by request type, then by individual request number, with a short recommendation and litigation rationale for each.
- The memo should conclude with a concise Recommended Actions section that tells counsel or the relevant business owner what to do next and on what timing.
- The formal responses should be captioned, numbered to match the requests, and drafted in a standard discovery-response style suitable for filing or service after local formatting is checked.
- Each formal response should clearly state: admitted, denied, unable to admit or deny after reasonable inquiry, objected to, or produced/withheld in part, as appropriate.
- When an objection is asserted, pair it with a substantive response to any non-objectionable portion whenever possible.
- When production is promised, identify the category of documents or ESI to be produced and avoid leaving the response ambiguous.
- When materials are withheld, say so expressly and maintain the objection consistently across the response set.
- Before finishing, confirm that each requested output file is fully drafted and non-empty, and that the responses are operative text rather than a mere summary of what would be done.
