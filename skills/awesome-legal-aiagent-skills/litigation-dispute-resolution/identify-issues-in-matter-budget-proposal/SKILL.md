# Skill: Identify Issues in Litigation Matter Budget Proposal

## 1. Subject-matter triage

- Read the governing billing guidelines, the proposed matter budget, any rate-change notice, any staffing/timekeeper notice, and the most recent actuals or forecast tied to the same matter.
- Determine whether there is only one budget period and one comparability baseline, or whether multiple phases, periods, or scenarios must be analyzed separately.
- If more than one phase, period, or scenario is in scope, enumerate each explicitly before analysis and assess them one by one against the same governing standards.
- Separate compliance defects from mere commercial judgment calls: the task is to identify issues, not to rewrite the budget unless the materials call for a corrective recommendation.

## 2. Failure modes the skill is correcting

- Reviewing the budget proposal without first anchoring to the governing billing guidelines that control rate approval, staffing approval, phase structure, and budget revisions.
- Comparing proposed figures to prior actuals without checking whether the case posture and phase are comparable.
- Treating a unilateral rate-change notice as approval when the guidelines require advance written consent or other express authorization.
- Missing a staffing addition that introduces new timekeepers outside the approved plan or above the authorized level.
- Flagging a budget increase without checking whether the increase is explained by changed case posture, new activity, or a guideline-compliant revision process.
- Failing to distinguish structural non-compliance with the budget form or submission process from substantive over-budgeting.
- Listing issues without tying each one to the governing authority, the relevant comparison point, and the client consequence.

## 3. Legal frameworks / domain conventions that apply

- The governing billing guidelines are the controlling authority for matter budgeting unless the source set expressly says otherwise.
- Rate changes are typically controlled by the guidelines’ approval provisions, any rate cap, and any advance notice or written consent requirement.
- Staffing changes are typically controlled by the guidelines’ timekeeper approval provisions and any restrictions on adding personnel or billing roles.
- Budget revision rules commonly govern when a reforecast is required, who must approve it, and whether overages can be incurred before approval.
- Phase-based budgets should be assessed by phase and by workload driver; a mismatch between phase allocation and expected activity can indicate poor planning, unsupported scope expansion, or over-resourcing.
- Prior-period actuals are a reasonableness benchmark, not a standalone approval standard; they matter only when the baseline phase and litigation posture are comparable.
- If the source documents contain a defined threshold, cap, notice period, approval requirement, or permitted variance, cite and apply that authority directly.
- Do not state a compliance conclusion without identifying the specific guideline provision or other controlling authority that supports it.

## 4. Analytical scaffolds

- Start with a standards-first read: identify the provisions governing rates, staffing, phase structure, revisions, and pre-approval.
- Build the issue list in categories:
  - rate compliance issues
  - unauthorized or unapproved timekeepers
  - phase allocation or workload mismatches
  - budget form or guideline-structure defects
  - revision, notice, or approval-process defects
- For each issue, perform three closing moves:
  - quantify the deviation or exposure using the figures or threshold in the source materials;
  - cross-reference the interacting guideline, notice, schedule, or prior-period actual;
  - state the downstream consequence for the client, including whether the problem creates approval risk, reimbursement risk, process risk, or planning risk.
- Where multiple timekeepers, phases, or periods appear, analyze each separately rather than collapsing them into a single composite observation.
- If the materials do not supply enough detail to compute a precise exposure, state the issue conservatively using the available figures and identify the missing input.
- Use the prior period only as a comparator where the case posture is materially similar; if the posture has changed, explain why the comparison is limited.
- Distinguish “unsupported,” “inconsistent with guidelines,” “not approved,” and “requires escalation” as separate findings when the materials support them.
- Confirm whether any rate increase, added resource, or reforecast was expressly approved before treating it as compliant.
- If a budget item appears disproportionate, connect the mismatch to the expected phase work and any operative guideline on staffing or phase budgeting.

## 5. Vertical / structural / temporal relationships

- Compare the proposed budget vertically against the controlling guideline hierarchy: guideline, then notice, then budget submission, then prior actuals.
- Compare horizontally across phases and timekeepers to identify uneven allocation, unauthorized additions, or unexplained cost shifts.
- Treat timing as material: pre-approval, advance notice, revised submission, and after-the-fact ratification are not interchangeable.
- Where the record includes multiple budget periods, compare each period only to the corresponding comparable period or phase.

## 6. Output structure conventions

- Produce a categorized issue memorandum suitable for filing as the requested document.
- Open with a short executive summary stating the overall compliance posture and the principal risk themes.
- Include a brief standards section identifying the governing authority used for the review.
- Provide a severity legend using an ordinal scale and apply it consistently to each issue.
- For each issue entry, include:
  - Severity
  - Issue title
  - Guideline or authority
  - Source comparison point
  - Quantified impact or exposure
  - Why it matters to the client
  - Recommended action
- Keep issues distinct; do not merge unrelated rate, staffing, phase, and process defects into a single paragraph.
- End with an explicit Recommended Actions section that assigns the action to the relevant role named in the source materials, or to outside counsel if no other role is identified, and ties the recommendation to the relevant deadline, approval event, or next budget milestone.
- If the materials support a summary table, place it near the front and use it to group issues by category and severity.
- Write the memorandum in concise business-legal prose, with enough detail to support escalation and internal decision-making.
