# Skill: Compare Estate Plan Against Federal Tax Exemption Schedules — Tax Exposure Memorandum

## 1. Subject-matter triage

- Treat the task as a document-to-law comparison: read the estate plan, gift history, and asset inventory as an integrated record, then test the plan against the federal transfer-tax regime in force on the relevant dates.
- Separate the work into: dispositive formula interpretation, taxable-gift history, gross-estate inclusion, GST sensitivity, retirement-account trust treatment, charitable provisions, and valuation-sensitive interests.
- Where the source set contains multiple instruments or amendments, read them in sequence and reconcile later provisions against earlier ones before drawing any tax conclusion.

## 2. Failure modes the skill is correcting

- Reading formula clauses as fixed when they may turn on competing interpretations with materially different tax results.
- Missing that a prior spouse’s unused exclusion may be unavailable, partially available, or time-barred depending on the death sequence and governing portability regime.
- Assuming life insurance or similar assets are excluded without confirming ownership, incidents of ownership, and beneficiary structure.
- Overlooking taxable gifts already used against the lifetime exemption and failing to align gift-tax history with estate-tax calculations.
- Treating a trust named for retirement assets as automatically eligible for favorable payout treatment without checking current trust-beneficiary requirements.
- Ignoring older drafting that may have been written against a prior exemption amount or prior distribution rule.
- Reaching conclusions on charitable, GST, or valuation issues without tying them to the operative statutory or regulatory standard.

## 3. Legal frameworks / domain conventions that apply

- Formula-funding clauses: interpret terms referencing a fixed amount, the exemption amount, or a share of residue under each plausible construction; test whether the clause funds a credit-shelter share, marital share, GST share, or other subtrust under current law.
- Federal estate and gift tax: apply the unified transfer-tax framework, including the basic exclusion amount, taxable gifts, adjusted taxable gifts, and generation-skipping transfer consequences.
- Portability: analyze whether unused exclusion from a deceased spouse can be elected and used, and whether the election timing and estate administration posture preserve that benefit.
- Estate inclusion: gross estate analysis must consider owned life insurance, retained powers, revocable transfers, and other incidents of ownership.
- Retirement-account beneficiary rules: a trust named as beneficiary of qualified retirement assets must be tested against the governing trust-look-through and distribution framework for the relevant account type and death date.
- Charitable remainder structures: deduction and validity depend on the governing valuation method, payout mechanics, and minimum remainder requirements.
- Closely held interests: valuation discount analysis should address minority, lack of marketability, entity-level restrictions, and any supportable range reflected by the source valuation materials.
- GST planning: formula clauses and trust allocations should be tested for exemption sufficiency, allocation coherence, and exposure to unintended inclusion or wastage.
- Governing authority: cite the controlling Internal Revenue Code, Treasury Regulations, and leading estate-tax authorities for each proposition rather than stating conclusions in the abstract.

## 4. Analytical scaffolds

1. Build a source map.
   - List every operative document, amendment, schedule, gift record, and inventory item.
   - Identify the date each document became effective and the transfer or death date that controls each tax question.

2. Enumerate each plausible interpretive path.
   - For every formula clause, list the plausible readings before computing consequences.
   - If only one reading is truly available, state why the text, sequence, or defined terms eliminate the alternatives.

3. Run a tax-exposure analysis for each path.
   - Measure the clause against the available exemption history, taxable gifts, and asset values in the record.
   - State the gross-estate, marital, charitable, GST, and trust-funding consequences under each interpretation.
   - Compare the result to the relevant federal exclusion or deduction framework and identify where the plan is exposed.

4. Trace exclusion history and portability.
   - Reconstruct prior use of exclusion and any deceased spouse unused exclusion issues from the source record.
   - Determine whether any election or lapse in timing affects the amount available today.
   - Tie the analysis to the timing of death, filing windows, and the law applicable on those dates.

5. Test estate inclusion assets.
   - For each insurance policy or analogous asset, identify owner, insured, beneficiary, and retained control rights.
   - For each closely held interest, test whether discounts are documented and whether the plan’s dispositive mechanics depend on those values.
   - For each retirement asset disposition, test whether the beneficiary design aligns with current trust-distribution rules.

6. Check older drafting against current law.
   - Flag provisions drafted under older exemption amounts, valuation assumptions, or distribution rules.
   - Assess whether the document still functions as intended or now produces mismatch, overfunding, underfunding, or unintended inclusion.

7. Identify practical post-mortem levers.
   - Note any spouse-level elections, disclaimer paths, fiduciary elections, or funding choices supported by the documents and source timeline.
   - State whether the record supports immediate corrective action or only retrospective risk assessment.

8. Tie every legal conclusion to authority.
   - For each issue, cite the statute, regulation, or leading authority that governs the rule applied.
   - Do not leave a conclusion unsupported by a named source of law.

## 5. Vertical / structural / temporal relationships

- Base instruments, amendments, and schedules operate together; later amendments control only within their subject matter and do not rewrite unrelated provisions.
- The relevant exemption amount, valuation standard, and distribution regime are determined at the operative transfer or death date, not the date the estate plan was drafted.
- Gift-tax history must be integrated into estate-tax exposure rather than analyzed as a separate ledger.
- If the record contains multiple dates of transfer, death, valuation, or election, analyze each one separately and then reconcile the overall tax effect.

## 6. Output structure conventions

- Write a memo-style product with an executive summary, issue-by-issue analysis, and a closing recommendations section.
- For each issue, state the controlling authority, the source documents implicated, the interpretive or factual uncertainty, and the resulting tax exposure or planning consequence.
- Where more than one interpretation is plausible, present the analysis under each interpretation before stating the practical implication.
- Include a concise summary of gross-estate components, taxable gifts used, portability implications, and any trust/funding mismatches.
- End with a Recommended Actions section that assigns next steps to the relevant fiduciary, advisor, or counsel and ties each step to a timing anchor from the record or the applicable filing/election window.
- Use clear headings and cite authorities inline; avoid free-standing conclusions that do not point back to the governing rule.
