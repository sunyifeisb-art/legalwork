# Skill: Draft Investigation Plan for Workplace Harassment and Retaliation Complaint

## 1. Subject-matter triage
- Treat the complaint, supporting company records, and any attached email or messaging materials as the operative record for planning.
- Determine whether the plan is being drafted for internal HR response, counsel-led investigation, or a hybrid process; the privilege posture and investigator selection depend on that posture.
- Identify whether the complaint includes harassment, retaliation, or both, and whether the allegations implicate managers, HR, or bystanders; tailor the witness map accordingly.
- Flag immediately any request for a neutral investigator, any prior complaint about the same respondent, or any preservation risk tied to electronic systems with deletion settings.

## 2. Failure modes the skill is correcting
- The plan assumes investigator neutrality without testing whether the proposed investigator has a prior relationship, reporting tie, or other credibility issue.
- The plan jumps to interviewing the respondent before the complainant’s account, documents, and corroborating witnesses are captured, creating avoidable contamination risk.
- The plan ignores preservation of email, chat, collaboration tools, shared drives, and other systems that may overwrite relevant evidence during the investigation.
- The plan omits review of earlier informal complaints or HR responses involving the same respondent, leaving the employer-defense analysis incomplete.
- The plan fails to preserve privilege from the outset, causing interview notes, draft reports, or internal communications to be treated as ordinary business records.
- The plan neglects possible insurance notice obligations or other reporting deadlines triggered by a formal complaint.
- The plan lists tasks without assigning an owner, sequence, or timing anchor, making the investigation unworkable.

## 3. Legal frameworks / domain conventions that apply
- Investigator neutrality: use an investigator with no material prior personal or professional relationship that would reasonably call impartiality into question; document the neutrality assessment in the memorandum.
- Employer response framework: assess whether the employer acted with reasonable care to prevent and correct alleged harassment or retaliation, including whether reporting channels existed and were usable.
- Retaliation framework: evaluate whether the complaint, timing, supervisory actions, discipline, scheduling changes, or access restrictions suggest protected activity followed by materially adverse treatment.
- Preservation duty: once a complaint or threatened claim is reasonably anticipated, preserve potentially relevant information and suspend deletion or overwrite processes for covered custodians and systems.
- Privilege doctrine: when counsel directs the investigation, structure the process to support attorney-client privilege and work-product protection; limit distribution and label communications accordingly.
- Insurance notice: review any employment-practices or management-liability policy for prompt notice requirements tied to a formal complaint or potential claim.
- Common investigation practice: document each factual step, maintain a clean interview chronology, and separate fact-finding from conclusions.

## 4. Analytical scaffolds
- Scope first: define the alleged conduct, time period, locations, systems, and actors to be covered; distinguish the harassment theory from the retaliation theory.
- Investigator selection:
  - identify the proposed investigator;
  - test for prior relationships, reporting lines, or prior involvement;
  - state whether the investigator may proceed or should be replaced.
- Complaint-history review:
  - search for prior informal complaints, manager escalations, exit interviews, HR notes, or hotline contacts involving the same respondent;
  - note how each prior matter was handled;
  - assess the defense significance of any pattern, prior notice, or corrective action gap.
- Preservation plan:
  - identify relevant custodians, repositories, devices, and platforms;
  - list likely sources of emails, chats, texts, shared drives, badge/access records, calendars, HR files, and performance records;
  - direct a litigation hold and suspension of auto-delete or retention overwrite features where needed.
- Document collection plan:
  - specify which records are needed before interviews and which can be gathered in parallel;
  - assign collection responsibility to counsel, HR, IT, or the business lead as appropriate.
- Witness sequencing:
  - interview the complainant first unless there is a documented reason not to;
  - collect and review corroborating documents next;
  - then interview corroborating witnesses identified from the record;
  - interview the respondent last.
- Interview protocol:
  - define the core topics for each witness category;
  - include confidentiality and non-retaliation reminders;
  - preserve notes in privileged form if counsel is directing the process.
- Timeline:
  - set an opening date for the hold and initial collection;
  - set target dates for interviews, interim check-ins, and the final memorandum;
  - include any insurance notice deadline or external reporting milestone.
- Recommendation framing:
  - each recommendation should identify the action, the responsible role, and the timing anchor;
  - keep the recommendation operational, not abstract.

## 5. Vertical / structural / temporal relationships
- When multiple allegations, respondents, locations, or time periods are present, separate them before analysis and assign the relevant witness set and document set to each.
- When conduct spans before and after the complaint date, distinguish pre-complaint harassment evidence from post-complaint retaliation evidence.
- When the same manager is implicated in both the underlying conduct and the response, treat the response chain as a separate investigative thread.
- When records exist across HR, IT, and line management, note which source controls each fact and which source is corroborative.
- When preservation, insurance notice, and interview timing collide, prioritize hold issuance and evidence stabilization before substantive interviews beyond the complainant.

## 6. Output structure conventions
- Draft as a privileged attorney-client investigation plan memorandum.
- Use a conventional memorandum shape: purpose and scope, privilege and confidentiality statement, investigator selection and neutrality assessment, complaint-history review, preservation and litigation hold, document collection, witness plan and sequencing, interview protocols, timeline, insurance notice, and action items.
- Mark the memorandum privileged on each page or in the header/footer if the format permits.
- Write in direct planning language, not in narrative report style; the memorandum should tell the reader what to do, in what order, and why.
- Include an explicit recommended actions section at the end with imperative steps, responsible roles, and timing anchors.
- Do not speculate beyond the provided complaint file and company records; identify information gaps and state what must be obtained to close them.
- If the investigation plan relies on a legal proposition, name the governing rule, doctrine, or policy basis rather than stating the conclusion alone.
