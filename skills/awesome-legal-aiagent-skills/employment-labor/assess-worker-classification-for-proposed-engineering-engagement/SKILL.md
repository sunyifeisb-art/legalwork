# Skill: Assess Worker Classification for Proposed Engineering Engagement

## 1. Subject-matter triage

- Determine which jurisdictions and legal regimes are actually in play before analyzing status; do not assume one worker-classification test governs all uses.
- If the source set contains a draft agreement, SOW, onboarding materials, org charts, timekeeping rules, or prior employment history, treat each as a separate input to the classification analysis.
- If the worker has any prior employee relationship with the same company in a similar role, elevate scrutiny and test whether the new label changes substance or only form.

## 2. Failure modes the skill is correcting

- Analyst applies only one control-based framework and misses that wage-and-hour, tax, benefits, and state-law regimes may apply different tests.
- Analyst treats the consulting agreement as dispositive and underweights actual work structure, supervision, and operational integration.
- Analyst ignores evidence that the worker is embedded in the company’s business, such as employer tools, workspace, core-hour availability, or direct reporting.
- Analyst fails to account for prior employment in the same or a substantially similar role, which can signal re-labeling rather than a genuine independent relationship.
- Analyst stops at risk identification without proposing structural remediation that could materially reduce misclassification exposure.

## 3. Legal frameworks / domain conventions that apply

- Identify the governing classification test for each relevant use: federal wage-and-hour law commonly turns on the Fair Labor Standards Act and the Department of Labor’s economic reality framework; tax status often turns on common-law control principles; some states apply an ABC-style presumption or an equivalent statutory test; benefits and agency regimes may use still other standards.
- Where multiple regimes apply, analyze each separately and note when the same facts point in different directions under different tests.
- Under ABC-style regimes, the hiring party generally bears the burden of showing the worker is free from control, performs work outside the usual course of business or outside the place of business as required by the local test, and customarily operates an independently established trade or business; use the jurisdiction’s statutory formulation, not a generic shorthand, when available.
- Under the economic reality framework, focus on control, opportunity for profit or loss, investment, permanence, and skill; none is dispositive standing alone.
- Under common-law control frameworks, the key question is whether the company has the right to control the manner and means of performance, not merely the end product.
- Integration facts are strong employee indicators: company-provided equipment, assigned workspace, mandatory availability during core business hours, manager-directed work allocation, required status reporting, and use of company systems as a functional employee substitute.
- Prior employment in a substantially similar role raises risk that the new arrangement is a nominal reclassification, especially where the workstream, supervision, hours, or tools remain materially unchanged.
- Misclassification consequences may include payroll tax exposure, overtime or minimum-wage liability, statutory penalties, benefit-plan exposure, and downstream wage statement or recordkeeping issues.
- Cite the controlling authority for each proposition relied on, using the applicable statute, regulation, rule, or leading case.

## 4. Analytical scaffolds

- Start by stating the regimes to be analyzed and whether the facts support one or multiple classification tests.
- For each applicable regime, walk the factors in a fixed sequence: control, integration, economic dependence, independence/business ownership, and prior relationship history if relevant.
- Separate contract language from operational reality; if the paper structure conflicts with the day-to-day arrangement, the day-to-day arrangement usually drives risk.
- Treat each integration fact as an affirmative employee signal rather than a neutral convenience.
- Assess whether the worker has a genuine independent business: other clients, business entity, tools and insurance, entrepreneurial risk, autonomy over schedule, and the ability to hire helpers or substitute performance where legally and operationally relevant.
- Where the worker is expected to be available like a staff engineer, to use company systems as the primary work platform, or to report into the company’s management chain, explain how those facts change the classification analysis.
- If the worker previously performed similar work as an employee, compare the old and proposed arrangements side by side and identify whether the proposed structure materially changes control and independence.
- Conclude each regime with a risk judgment tied to the governing test, then synthesize the results into an overall classification risk assessment.
- If risk is elevated, propose concrete structural changes: narrow control over methods, remove required core-hour availability, avoid furnishing routine tools or workspace, permit off-hours and outside-client work where feasible, and document true project-based independence.
- Make recommendations practical and operational, not abstract; tie them to the company role that must implement them and to the point in the engagement when they should occur.

## 5. Vertical / structural / temporal relationships

- Evaluate the relationship across the life cycle of the engagement: onboarding, day-to-day performance, reporting cadence, work location, payment structure, and offboarding.
- Distinguish between pre-engagement drafting risk and post-signature operational risk; both matter, but actual practice carries greater weight.
- If the materials include a prior employment period, compare that period to the proposed contractor period in a before/after sequence: role, supervision, hours, tools, location, and business independence.
- If the worker is embedded in a broader engineering team, assess whether the role is functionally staff augmentation rather than a discrete outside-service arrangement.
- If any headcount, benefits, or payroll decision depends on the classification result, flag timing sensitivity so the business does not implement a status change prematurely.

## 6. Output structure conventions

- Format the output as a risk memorandum addressed to in-house counsel or the responsible business team.
- Use industry-conventional headings rather than a rigid checklist; organize by governing regime, then by factor, then by conclusion.
- Include a short executive summary with an overall risk rating.
- Define an ordinal severity scale once if listing issues or remediation items, and apply it consistently.
- For each issue or factor, state the governing authority, the factual basis from the source materials, and the practical consequence of the fact pattern.
- Include a remediation section with specific, actionable steps and assign each step to a responsible role with a timing anchor.
- End with a Recommended Actions section that uses imperative verbs, identifies the responsible role, and ties timing to a milestone or urgency cue from the materials.
- If the memo references multiple legal regimes, make the regime-specific conclusion explicit so the reader can see where the risk is concentrated.
