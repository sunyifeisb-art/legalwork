# Skill: Review Counterparty CEO Employment Agreement for Acquisition Target

## 1. Subject-matter triage

This is an acquisition-context review of a target CEO’s employment agreement, any amendment, and any related side arrangement. Read the agreement set as a package and assume provisions may differ across documents unless an amendment clearly states that it supersedes the earlier term. Use the acquiror’s perspective throughout: identify inherited payment obligations, post-close operational constraints, consent rights, acceleration mechanics, and integration friction.

First enumerate the document set and the live questions it raises:
1. Base employment agreement.
2. Amendment(s) or restatement(s).
3. Any consulting, equity, severance, or retention attachment referenced by either document.
4. Any acquisition term or diligence guidance that changes how the agreement should be read.

Treat each separate document and each separate trigger or restriction as its own item before drawing conclusions.

## 2. Failure modes the skill is correcting

- Analyst recites a change-in-control trigger but does not test it against the contemplated deal structure or explain the resulting payment or vesting consequences.
- Analyst notes a consulting tail, noncompete, or consent right without stating whether it binds the acquiror, how long it lasts, or what it costs to unwind.
- Analyst flags a clawback clause without comparing it to the applicable public-company compensation recovery regime if future listing or public status is possible.
- Analyst reviews IP ownership language without testing whether a passive-investment carve-out or similar exception would permit competitive involvement.
- Analyst identifies a breach cure period, fee shift, or excise-tax protection but does not connect it to the integration timeline, dispute posture, or transaction closing mechanics.
- Analyst treats an amendment as impliedly controlling without checking for express supremacy language or conflict resolution.
- Analyst describes issues in the abstract without ranking severity or stating the downstream deal consequence.

## 3. Legal frameworks / domain conventions that apply

- Change-in-control analysis: identify the trigger mechanics, then test whether the contemplated transaction form, closing ownership position, or control shift satisfies the definition and activates severance, vesting, or other benefits. Use the controlling contractual definition, not a shorthand label.
- Amendment hierarchy: compare the base agreement and each amendment clause by clause; apply the later text only to the extent the documents actually modify the earlier term.
- Consulting and transition obligations: post-termination services can become an inherited operating cost if they are not freely terminable by the acquiror or if they lock in minimum compensation, exclusivity, or availability.
- Clawback and recovery language: compare any repayment or forfeiture provision to the applicable public-company compensation recovery standard, including Securities Exchange Act of 1934, Section 10D, and Exchange Act Rule 10D-1 where relevant.
- Cure periods: a short employer-cure window can compress the company’s response time during diligence, closing, and integration; assess against the breach type and the likely timing of post-close change.
- Attorney fee shifting: fee-shift clauses change settlement leverage and enforcement economics; assess mutuality and whether the clause creates a one-way advantage.
- Excise-tax or gross-up protection: transaction-based payments may implicate Section 280G and Section 4999 of the Internal Revenue Code; identify whether any cutback, shareholder approval, or other mitigation mechanic is built in.
- IP assignment carve-outs: passive-investment exceptions should be tested for breadth, ownership threshold, and permitted activity so they do not quietly allow meaningful competitive involvement.
- Restrictive covenant enforceability: noncompete, nonsolicit, confidentiality, and invention-assignment terms should be read against the governing state-law framework and any public-policy constraints that may affect enforceability at or after closing.
- Contract interpretation: use the agreement’s own defined terms, incorporation provisions, and conflict clauses before resorting to assumptions.

## 4. Analytical scaffolds

- Agreement set comparison:
  1. Identify every operative document.
  2. For each material provision, compare base text to amendment text.
  3. State whether the later text narrows, expands, or leaves unchanged the acquiror’s burden.
  4. Flag any ambiguity in supremacy or coexistence.

- Change-in-control and severance analysis:
  1. Extract the full trigger definition.
  2. Test the contemplated transaction against each element.
  3. Identify any acceleration, severance, bonus, vesting, or benefit continuation that follows.
  4. State whether the obligation is automatic, discretionary, or conditioned on a release, notice, or other act.

- Consulting, transition, and post-close services analysis:
  1. Identify duration, hours, compensation, scope, and termination rights.
  2. Determine whether the acquiror can terminate without penalty.
  3. Assess whether the commitment becomes an ongoing transaction cost or integration constraint.
  4. Tie the issue to the anticipated post-close operating model.

- Clawback and repayment analysis:
  1. Identify the trigger for recovery.
  2. Compare the contract language to the applicable public-company recovery standard.
  3. Flag missing or inconsistent mechanisms that may need amendment before public status, listing, or governance refresh.
  4. Note whether recovery is mandatory, discretionary, or limited to specific misconduct or restatement events.

- Cure period and breach-right analysis:
  1. Identify notice and cure mechanics.
  2. Measure the cure period against the likely timing of integration-related changes.
  3. Explain how the period affects good-reason, termination, or dispute leverage.
  4. Flag especially short periods as operationally brittle.

- Fee shifting and enforcement analysis:
  1. Identify who pays fees and in what circumstances.
  2. Determine whether the clause is mutual or one-sided.
  3. Explain the strategic effect on enforcement, settlement, and litigation posture.

- Excise-tax mitigation analysis:
  1. Identify whether transaction payments are cut back, grossed up, or conditioned on approval.
  2. Check whether the mechanism coordinates with closing timing and any shareholder vote requirement.
  3. State the economic consequence if the provision is triggered.

- IP carve-out analysis:
  1. Identify any passive-investment or noncontrol exception.
  2. Measure the ownership threshold and the scope of permitted activity.
  3. Assess whether the exception allows meaningful participation in a competing enterprise.

- Issue writing discipline:
  1. Start with the provision.
  2. Explain the acquiror concern.
  3. Cross-reference the interacting clause or document.
  4. Tie the point to a threshold, term, trigger, or duration from the source.
  5. State the downstream consequence for the transaction.

## 5. Vertical / structural / temporal relationships

The analysis is acquisition-sensitive and must be read across time:
- Pre-close: identify consents, notices, breach cures, termination rights, and transaction-triggered payments that may affect signing or closing.
- At close: identify vesting, severance, bonus, retention, or change-in-control effects that may be triggered by the deal structure.
- Post-close: identify ongoing consulting, restrictive covenants, indemnities, repurchase rights, confidentiality, IP obligations, or fee-shift exposure that will shape integration.

Map each issue to the relevant vertical relationship:
- Agreement to amendment.
- Employment agreement to equity or incentive documents.
- Contract terms to contemplated transaction form.
- Contract obligations to integration timeline.
- Internal diligence guidance to market position or negotiated guardrails.

When more than one transaction structure is possible, analyze the provision against each relevant structure rather than assuming a single outcome.

## 6. Output structure conventions

Write the deliverable as an issue identification memorandum organized by severity.

Use a defined ordinal severity scale at the outset, such as:
- Critical
- High
- Medium
- Low

Apply the same scale consistently to every issue and give a one-line reason for the rating.

For each issue, include:
- The governing provision or clause.
- The acquiror concern.
- The cross-reference to the related clause, amendment, schedule, or transaction term.
- The threshold, trigger, duration, or other source-based measure that makes the issue concrete.
- The downstream consequence for the acquiror.
- A recommended protective measure or negotiating position.
- A severity rating.

Include a concise summary table of all issues and recommended responses.

End with a Recommended Actions section that gives concrete next steps in imperative form, names the responsible role from the source set when available, and ties each action to a transaction milestone or other timing anchor.

If the source materials identify a controlling statute, regulation, or other authority for a proposition, cite that authority by name and section or rule. Do not state a legal conclusion without naming the authority that supports it.
