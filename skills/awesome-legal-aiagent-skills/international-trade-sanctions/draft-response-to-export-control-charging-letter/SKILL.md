# Skill: Draft Response to BIS Export Control Charging Letter

## 1. Subject-matter triage

- Identify the charging authority, the cited export-control provisions, the respondent entities, the charged conduct, the procedural posture, and the response deadline.
- Separate substantive violations from technical or ministerial defects, and separate penalty issues from settlement posture and remedial narrative.
- If the record contains multiple charges, incidents, shipment cycles, classification events, or disclosure periods, enumerate them first and analyze each on its own facts rather than collapsing them into a single narrative.
- If the materials support only one charging theory or one violation cluster, say so expressly and explain why the remainder are duplicative or unsupported.

## 2. Failure modes the skill is correcting

- Addressing the charges without a charge-by-charge defense, so the response reads as a general plea rather than a targeted administrative filing.
- Failing to tie mitigation to the actual discovery pathway, escalation timeline, disclosure posture, and remedial steps reflected in the record.
- Treating every allegation as equally serious instead of distinguishing willful conduct, negligent conduct, and purely technical violations that caused limited or no harm.
- Accepting the penalty figure in the charging letter without independently checking the controlling statutory and regulatory penalty authority.
- Neglecting to confront any requested denial order with a proportionality analysis grounded in the seriousness of the conduct, any security impact, and the respondent’s history.
- Offering conclusions without citing the controlling statutory, regulatory, or guideline authority for each material proposition.
- Drafting defenses in a way that loses the settlement ask; the response should preserve advocacy while still presenting a credible resolution path.

## 3. Legal frameworks / domain conventions that apply

- Civil export-control charging responses are framed by the cited Export Administration Regulations, the applicable enforcement provisions in the Export Control Reform Act and related penalty authority, and BIS enforcement guidance.
- Mitigation is strongest when violations were discovered through internal controls, compliance review, or independent audit, then escalated promptly, disclosed appropriately, and followed by documented remediation.
- Willfulness turns on intent and awareness, not merely mistake; the response should distinguish deliberate evasion from good-faith error, process failure, or negligent oversight under the governing enforcement standard.
- Penalty analysis should be anchored to the current civil maximum under the controlling authority, including any inflation-adjusted notice or other authoritative update, and should reconcile that authority with the charging letter’s stated ceiling.
- Denial relief, where sought, should be tested against the seriousness of the conduct, the presence or absence of national-security risk, prior violations, and the adequacy of lesser sanctions under the enforcement framework.
- Technical violations should be isolated and treated separately when they created no material diversion risk, no prohibited end use, and no meaningful harm.
- Remedial measures are not decorative; they are mitigation evidence when tied to training, screening, classification, escalation, audits, discipline, controls, and process redesign.

## 4. Analytical scaffolds

1. For each charge, identify the cited provision, the factual predicate, the best defense, and the remedy sought for that specific allegation.
2. Build the mitigation narrative around the source of discovery, the chronology of escalation, the timing and completeness of any disclosure, and the concrete remediation already undertaken.
3. Verify the current civil maximum from the controlling authority, identify any discrepancy with the charging letter, and state the aggregate exposure using only the figures supplied in the source materials.
4. Analyze willfulness separately from negligence by testing for intent, concealment, awareness of the rule, and evidence of deliberate circumvention.
5. If a denial order is requested or threatened, analyze whether the record supports such relief under the applicable enforcement standard and whether a narrower sanction would address the conduct.
6. Isolate any charge that is merely technical, identify why the practical harm is limited, and argue for reduced treatment relative to more serious allegations.
7. Convert each remediation item into mitigation: state what changed, when it changed, and how it reduces recurrence risk.
8. End with a settlement position that connects the defenses and mitigation to a concrete requested resolution, including any penalty rationale.

## 5. Vertical / structural / temporal relationships

- Track the chronology from first detection, through escalation, internal review, disclosure, agency contact, and post-violation remediation.
- Distinguish conduct that occurred before corrective action from conduct after controls were strengthened; post-remediation conduct should be treated differently where the record supports it.
- Where one document’s facts qualify another’s allegation, cross-reference them directly so the reader can see how the chronology changes the legal significance of the charge.
- If the record includes multiple respondent roles, shipment or transaction phases, or compliance owners, assign responsibility precisely rather than attributing all facts to one actor.
- Tie each proposed concession or settlement term to the procedural stage and the regulatory objective it is meant to serve.

## 6. Output structure conventions

- Draft a formal administrative response addressed to BIS in professional agency style.
- Use an introduction, factual background, charge-by-charge response, mitigation and remediation section, penalty/settlement discussion, and conclusion or requested relief section.
- Address each charge individually with: the governing authority, the factual defense, the mitigation point, and the requested disposition.
- Cite controlling authority by name and section for every legal proposition relied on; do not state conclusions without anchoring them to the governing rule, regulation, statute, or guideline.
- When making penalty arguments, include the current authority, the respondent’s position on exposure, and the requested settlement figure or term, with a short rationale.
- If the filing recommends action items for the client team, end with an explicit Recommended Actions section that assigns each action to a role and a timing anchor.
- Write the response as the final filing itself, not as notes, not as an outline, and not as a summary of what would be filed.
