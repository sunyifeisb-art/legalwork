# Skill: Compare OFAC Pre-Penalty Notice Factors Against Enforcement Guidelines for Sanctions Violation Response

## 1. Subject-matter triage

- Treat the notice, the enforcement guidelines, the underlying transaction record, and any disclosure or remediation materials as a single comparison set.
- Identify whether the matter involves one violation theory or multiple theories; if multiple, separate them before analysis and do not merge distinct methodologies.
- Determine the penalty posture first: apparent violation type, disclosure posture, and whether the notice is using a per-violation, transaction-value, or other published approach.
- Confirm whether the analysis is intended to challenge the amount, the factual predicates, the culpability factors, or all three; structure the memorandum accordingly.

## 2. Failure modes the skill is correcting

- Accepting the stated penalty methodology without independently verifying the applicable statutory maximum figure against the current inflation-adjusted amount.
- Failing to identify when the penalty calculation does not follow the published methodology for the relevant violation classification and disclosure posture.
- Accepting factual characterizations in the notice without verifying each against the underlying transaction records, internal investigation, and disclosure materials.
- Treating aggravating statements as established facts when the record only supports inference or partial support.
- Missing the difference between a mathematical discrepancy and a legal methodology error.
- Omitting a negotiation posture after identifying corrected penalty inputs and mitigation facts.

## 3. Legal frameworks / domain conventions that apply

- Penalty methodology comparison: identify the enforcement-guideline method that applies to the violation classification and disclosure posture at issue, then test whether the notice follows that method rather than a simplified or incomplete alternative.
- Per-violation statutory maximum adjusted for inflation: identify the current inflation-adjusted civil penalty maximum from the most recent published source; challenge any notice relying on a stale, inconsistent, or unsupported figure.
- Independent penalty arithmetic verification: compare the penalty implicit in the notice to the stated violation count, the transaction-value method, and the applicable maximum; any arithmetic inconsistency may reflect an error, undisclosed adjustment, or unsupported assumption that should be flagged.
- Egregiousness factor assessment: evaluate the stated aggravating and mitigating factors against the record, including knowledge, management involvement, repetition, harm to sanctions objectives, remediation, and compliance-history considerations; a lack of record support for an aggravating factor should be analyzed as a challenge point.
- Clean enforcement history as a mitigating factor: if the record reflects no prior enforcement actions, treat that as a mitigation point and evaluate whether the notice gives it appropriate weight in the overall penalty analysis.
- Controlling authority should be cited by name and section when available: the sanctions regulations, the governing statute, the enforcement guidelines, and any published civil-penalty adjustment source.
- Use the source documents’ own terminology for violation types, risk factors, and mitigation unless that terminology is inconsistent with the governing authority.

## 4. Analytical scaffolds

1. Identify the applicable penalty methodology for each violation theory and test whether the notice deviates from the published framework.
2. Verify the per-violation statutory maximum figure against the current inflation-adjusted amount and use that figure in any recalculation.
3. Independently calculate the transaction-value method penalty, where applicable, and compare the result to the notice's proposed amount.
4. For each factual assertion in the notice, verify against the transaction log, internal investigation, and disclosure materials; identify any factual error with source support.
5. Evaluate each aggravating and mitigating factor against the record; note where the record supports a non-egregious or otherwise mitigated characterization.
6. Separate methodology defects from factual defects and from discretionary weighting disputes; each requires a different challenge posture.
7. When more than one transaction, period, party, or violation theory is in scope, enumerate them explicitly before analysis and apply the scaffold once per item.
8. Measure each issue against the relevant source-document quantity, date, exposure, or transaction set; then state the interaction with any other notice factor or record document.
9. For every issue, close the analysis with consequence: effect on penalty amount, settlement leverage, disclosure posture, or compliance remediation framing.
10. State a negotiation target or recommended settlement posture based on the corrected analysis and rationale.

## 5. Vertical / structural / temporal relationships

- Distinguish between conduct-period facts, notice-date assertions, and post-incident remediation; do not let later remediation be used to rewrite the original factual record.
- Distinguish transaction-level issues from account-level or entity-level issues; if the notice aggregates them, test whether aggregation is authorized by the governing methodology.
- If the record contains multiple settlements, disclosures, or internal reviews, treat them as temporally ordered inputs and identify whether the notice relies on an earlier or later version.
- When the notice cites management knowledge, approvals, or escalations, trace the timing to the relevant transaction window and confirm whether the chronology supports the stated culpability inference.
- Where a factor depends on repetition or pattern, test whether the cited events are truly separate, contemporaneous, or functionally duplicative.

## 6. Output structure conventions

- Produce a penalty comparison memorandum, not a generic issue list.
- Open with an executive summary stating the corrected penalty posture, the principal challenge themes, and the settlement objective.
- Use a clear issue-by-issue format with a uniform severity label for each issue, such as Critical / High / Medium / Low, defined once at the top.
- For each issue, include: the notice position, the challenge basis with controlling authority, the corrected analysis, the quantitative or record-based scale of the issue, the cross-reference to the interacting document or factor, and the downstream consequence.
- Distinguish at least three categories of issues in the body: methodology errors, factual errors, and mitigation / aggravation weighting errors.
- Include a recalculation section that shows the corrected base, the governing method applied, and the resulting penalty range or settlement anchor.
- End with a Recommended Actions block that uses imperative verbs, identifies the responsible role, and provides a timing anchor tied to the response or settlement process.
- If the record supports a cleaner negotiating position than the notice suggests, state it directly and explain what documentation or correction would strengthen it.
- Avoid unsupported conclusions; every legal proposition should be tied to a governing rule, regulation, statute, or published enforcement guidance.
