# Skill: Corporate Bylaws vs. Best Practice Governance Guidelines Gap Analysis

## 2. Failure modes the skill is correcting

- Treating a governance change as suitable for board-level action when the analysis calls for a more durable governing-document update, or vice versa, without first assessing the required implementation pathway and any reversal risk
- Failing to cross-reference existing commitments already made by the company against the remaining governance gaps, which may result in recommending actions the company has already undertaken or is already obligated to complete
- Missing governance guideline requirements that are not commonly found in standard bylaw checklists, such as emergency-bylaw concepts, annual independence questionnaire obligations, and a designated shareholder engagement contact role
- Collapsing multiple governance topics into a single narrative instead of evaluating each guideline topic on its own terms and preserving the source-set interactions that change the recommended fix
- Stating that a provision is “market,” “best practice,” or “missing” without identifying the governing legal or policy authority that supports that conclusion

## 3. Legal frameworks / domain conventions that apply

- State corporate law controls whether a governance change belongs in bylaws, charter, committee charter, board action, or shareholder action; the analysis should distinguish durability from ease of adoption and identify the approval path that actually governs the change
- Special meeting rights must be analyzed under the applicable state corporate statute and the company’s governing documents to determine whether the right can be implemented by bylaw amendment alone or requires charter support
- Emergency bylaws should be tested against the applicable corporate statute authorizing emergency governance provisions for extraordinary disruption; where authorized, their absence may be a gap against best-practice guidelines even if not a baseline legal defect
- Chair and CEO separation should be evaluated by the instrument used to implement it and the stability of that instrument under the company’s amendment mechanics
- Majority voting and director resignation mechanics should be assessed against the bylaws, any policy or committee charter addressing resignations, and any applicable exchange rule or governance guideline calling for a defined board response process
- Exclusive forum provisions should be analyzed separately for internal affairs claims and federal securities claims, because those are distinct legal channels and may be treated differently in the governing documents
- Annual independence questionnaires, tenure limits, retirement ages, and shareholder engagement contacts are often governance-guideline concepts rather than mandatory statutory items; their legal significance depends on whether they are embodied in binding documents or adopted only as informal practice
- Exchange listing rules may already impose minimum committee-independence and related governance requirements; separate the regulatory floor from any additional guideline-driven enhancements
- Where the source set identifies a governing statute, regulation, exchange rule, or adopted governance policy, cite that authority by name and section or rule in the memo rather than relying on generic descriptions

## 4. Analytical scaffolds

- Start by sorting the source set into: the current bylaws, the best-practice guidelines, and any separate commitments or correspondence that already address a topic; treat the commitments as potentially binding constraints on timing and sequencing
- For each governance topic, assess four questions in order: what the current document says, what the guideline expects, whether the gap is legal, operational, or best-practice only, and what implementation vehicle is most durable
- When a topic can be fixed in more than one way, compare the options by amendment burden, reversibility, and fit with the company’s existing governance architecture; note when board or committee action is adequate and when a governing-document change is preferable
- Check whether an existing commitment already covers the issue; if so, state that the item is not a pure gap and identify any remaining implementation work or deadline pressure created by that commitment
- For each issue, identify the source-set interaction that matters most: a bylaw provision may be reinforced, limited, or superseded by another document, and that interaction should be stated explicitly
- Distinguish between a true omission, a partial implementation, and a provision that exists but uses a weaker mechanism than the guideline contemplates
- Where the guidelines call for a process or role rather than a hard rule, identify whether the company has an equivalent committee charter, board policy, or recurring practice; if not, frame the absence as a governance-design gap rather than a statutory defect
- Use a severity scale and apply it consistently across the memo, calibrated to governance impact and implementation urgency; define the scale once and use it uniformly
- If multiple topics are in play, enumerate them first and then analyze each one separately so no issue is blended into a generic catchall discussion
- End each issue discussion with a concrete recommendation that names the action, the responsible governance owner, and the timing anchor drawn from the source documents or the next relevant governance milestone

## 5. Vertical / structural / temporal relationships

- Existing commitments create independent obligations that can outrank a pure best-practice ordering; if a commitment addresses a topic, prioritize it even when the underlying guideline would otherwise be lower in the hierarchy
- A board policy can be an interim solution, but a bylaws amendment is more durable and harder to reverse; state when the memo is recommending one as a stopgap and the other as the final fix
- Exchange listing requirements set a floor, not a ceiling; do not treat compliance with listing rules as resolving a broader guideline gap unless the guideline and the rule are truly coextensive
- Temporal sequencing matters when a change depends on committee review, board approval, or shareholder action; identify the order of steps and any prerequisite action before recommending final implementation
- Where the source documents include a date, deadline, or milestone, tie the recommended action to that timing rather than using abstract urgency language alone

## 6. Output structure conventions

- Draft a memorandum organized by governance topic or guideline subject, not as a simple document-by-document summary
- Precede the issue analysis with a short severity legend, then apply the same ordinal label to every issue entry
- For each issue entry, include: current status in the bylaws or related governance materials; the guideline expectation; the nature of the gap; the governing legal or policy authority supporting the analysis; the recommended implementation pathway; any cross-reference to an existing commitment; and the practical consequence of leaving the gap unaddressed
- Treat the source-set interaction as part of the analysis, not a footnote: note whether a separate document already implements, constrains, or partially resolves the issue
- Include a dedicated section explaining which items are already covered by existing commitments or other governance materials and which remain open
- Include a short section on exchange listing requirements as the baseline regulatory floor, separate from the best-practice comparison
- End with an explicit Recommended Actions block stating the action, the responsible role, and the timing anchor for each recommendation
- Use conventional memorandum language and avoid reproducing any internal rubric structure or checklist wording
