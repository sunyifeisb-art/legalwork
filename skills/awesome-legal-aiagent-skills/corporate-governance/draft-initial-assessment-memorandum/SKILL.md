# Skill: Initial Assessment Memorandum for FDA Warning Letter Response — Pharmaceutical Manufacturer

## 1. Subject-matter triage

- Treat the assignment as an urgent board-level risk memo, not a line-by-line recitation of the warning letter.
- Start by classifying the source set into: the warning letter, the inspectional record, quality-system documents, internal communications, commercial agreements, transaction documents, and any disclosure materials.
- Identify whether the company is public, whether any recall or supply interruption is underway or reasonably foreseeable, and whether any transaction or supply agreement could be implicated by the regulatory status.
- If multiple products, facilities, or time periods are implicated, enumerate them separately before analysis; do not blend distinct facilities, batches, or periods into one generalized assessment.

## 2. Failure modes the skill is correcting

- Summarizing violations without separating ordinary GMP deficiencies from conduct that may support fraud, adulteration, or criminal referral risk.
- Missing the significance of management awareness, budget/resource decisions, and delayed remediation as aggravating facts.
- Treating the matter as purely regulatory and failing to test commercial, transactional, and disclosure consequences in parallel.
- Writing a narrative memo that identifies problems but does not rank severity, anchor conclusions to authority, or end with concrete next steps.
- Collapsing multiple products, facilities, agreements, or disclosure triggers into a single undifferentiated assessment.
- Presenting legal conclusions without naming the governing statute, regulation, or doctrine that supports them.

## 3. Legal frameworks / domain conventions that apply

- Current good manufacturing practice for finished pharmaceuticals under 21 C.F.R. Parts 210 and 211 governs written procedures, deviations, investigations, stability, record review, computerized systems, and data integrity controls.
- An FDA warning letter is a formal enforcement communication based on significant violations; it is more consequential than inspectional observations and can affect import status, contracting, and counterparties.
- A drug may be adulterated under 21 U.S.C. § 351(a)(2)(B) when manufactured, processed, packed, or held under conditions that do not conform to cGMP; adulteration is the statutory basis for recall, seizure, and injunction theories.
- Data integrity failures, including alteration or invalidation of out-of-specification results, can support fraud theories and DOJ referral risk; the assessment should distinguish ordinary quality failures from intentional or reckless data manipulation.
- Consent decree risk turns on enforcement history, systemic quality failures, management knowledge, and the adequacy and timing of remediation; permanent injunction exposure should be assessed under the Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act and equitable enforcement practice.
- Transaction agreements often contain regulatory compliance representations, condition precedents, termination rights, covenants, and material adverse change provisions that may be triggered by a warning letter or elevated inspection status.
- Public-company disclosure obligations may arise under the federal securities laws and exchange rules if the event is material, if a recall is launched or likely, or if a known trend or uncertainty requires updating prior disclosure.
- Privilege strategy matters from the outset where criminal referral is plausible; work should be structured to preserve attorney-client privilege and work product protection under recognized doctrine.

## 4. Analytical scaffolds

- **Violation-by-violation review.** For each cited violation, state the governing citation, summarize the root-cause category, and identify whether the issue reflects procedure, execution, oversight, validation, training, or system design.
- **Severity grading.** Assign each issue an ordinal severity label defined once at the top of the memo, and apply that label consistently across violations, data-integrity issues, enforcement exposure, deal risk, and disclosure risk.
- **Authority-linked conclusions.** Every material conclusion should cite the relevant regulation, statute, doctrine, or agreement provision supporting it; do not infer consequences without naming the rule that makes the consequence plausible.
- **Data-integrity referral assessment.** Identify the specific conduct, the affected records or tests, the frequency or pattern, and whether the evidence suggests intentional alteration, reckless disregard, or merely poor controls.
- **Management-awareness timeline.** Build a chronology of when leadership knew of the issue, what they understood, what resources were allocated or withheld, and how those facts affect the good-faith and remediation narrative.
- **Consent decree probability assessment.** Weigh prior enforcement history, scope of deficiencies, recurring nature, data-integrity issues, management knowledge, and continued distribution or release decisions.
- **Agreement-trigger analysis.** For each potentially affected agreement, identify the clause type, the triggering fact, any notice or cure mechanics, and the operational consequence if rights are exercised.
- **Disclosure assessment.** Separate immediate disclosure duties from follow-on amendment or update issues, and distinguish legal obligation from prudential investor-relations strategy.
- **Exposure modeling.** Break the worst-case exposure into remediation, external counsel and monitoring, recall or market withdrawal, supply disruption, enforcement proceedings, and transaction fallout; state the drivers of each component rather than only a total.
- **Issue-closing discipline.** For each issue, tie the point to a measurable source fact, note the interacting document or provision, and state the downstream operational, regulatory, litigation, or transactional consequence.

## 5. Vertical / structural / temporal relationships

- Link the regulatory response, internal investigation, and external messaging so that public statements do not undercut a candid legal assessment or create inconsistency with transaction representations.
- Treat escalation as temporal: inspection findings may mature into a warning letter, which may mature into recall, import restriction, consent decree, securities disclosure, or deal trigger consequences.
- If the same facts affect both enforcement risk and commercial rights, present the regulatory and contractual analyses together so the board sees the shared factual foundation.
- If criminal referral is a realistic possibility, recommend a privilege-protective investigation structure before substantive interviews, document collection, or third-party outreach expand the record.
- Where several facilities, products, or agreements are implicated, analyze each separately and then state any portfolio-level or enterprise-level aggregation effects.

## 6. Output structure conventions

- Draft as a privileged board-level initial assessment memorandum, clearly marked attorney-client privileged and prepared in anticipation of litigation.
- Use an executive-summary opening, then a structured body that covers: the warning-letter and violation overview; the severity and data-integrity assessment; the enforcement, criminal, contractual, and disclosure exposure analysis; the response strategy; and the financial exposure estimate.
- Present each issue with a severity label, a short authority-based conclusion, and a concise consequence statement.
- End with a dedicated Recommended Actions block that uses imperative verbs, identifies the responsible role or function, and ties each action to an immediate deadline or regulatory milestone.
- Keep the memo practical and decision-oriented; it should equip the board to direct remediation, preserve privilege, and manage external risk.
