# Skill: H-1B RFE Issue Identification Memorandum

## 1. Subject-matter triage
- Treat the RFE notice as the organizing spine, but do not let it define the full scope of review.
- Review the entire petition record for issues in the petitioner’s own materials that may affect response strategy, record integrity, or credibility.
- This task is an issue-spotting and advisory exercise, not a merits brief; the output should identify concerns, not argue them away.
- If multiple RFE grounds are present, enumerate them first in the order presented and analyze each separately.

## 2. Failure modes the skill is correcting
- The analysis stops at the agency’s stated concerns and misses independent defects in the filed record.
- Documents from the petitioner are assumed accurate simply because they were submitted, even when they contain internal errors or contradictions.
- Filing chronology problems are overlooked even when the record creates a mismatch between a prerequisite filing, a status description, and the petition narrative.
- The response strategy is treated as identical for all problems, without distinguishing technical deficiencies from broader accuracy or credibility concerns.
- The memorandum identifies concerns without ranking their seriousness or stating what practical consequences they create.

## 3. Legal frameworks / domain conventions that apply
- Apply the H-1B framework implicated by the record, including specialty-occupation analysis, employer-employee/control analysis, job-description alignment, organizational-consistency review, and sequence/prerequisite compliance where relevant.
- Use the petition record’s own chronology as a governing reference point; if the documents describe a prerequisite filing, status, or event, test those descriptions against the petition timeline and each other.
- A discrepancy in the petitioner’s own submission can matter even if it is not the express basis of the RFE, because it may undercut the response, invite follow-up scrutiny, or require correction before submission.
- When a petitioner-facing document contains an error, assess whether it should be corrected directly in the RFE response, supported by an updated exhibit, or handled through a separate corrective filing strategy.
- State the controlling legal or regulatory authority for each proposition relied on, using the relevant statute, regulation, or recognized agency framework for the H-1B issue being discussed.

## 4. Analytical scaffolds
1. Parse the RFE notice: enumerate each agency-identified ground in the order presented and tie it to the evidence the agency appears to be missing or questioning.
2. Inventory the record: separate the petition support letter, prerequisite filings, organizational materials, job materials, beneficiary materials, and any chronologically sensitive exhibits.
3. Review each RFE ground: for each one, identify the issue, the governing H-1B element, the record documents that bear on it, and the practical response posture.
4. Check petitioner-authored documents independently: read them for factual accuracy, internal consistency, date/order alignment, title or role consistency, and consistency with supporting exhibits.
5. Verify chronology: test whether the record reflects the correct sequence of events and whether any document describes that sequence inconsistently or ambiguously.
6. Classify each issue: distinguish a technical defect from an accuracy, inconsistency, or credibility concern; then assign a severity level and explain the basis for that ranking.
7. Close each issue: state the evidentiary hook, the cross-document interaction, and the downstream consequence for the petition or response strategy.
8. Recommend the fix: identify whether the issue should be corrected in the response package, supported with a clarifying exhibit, or handled outside the response.

## 5. Vertical / structural / temporal relationships
- Track relationships across time, not just within a single document: filing date, prerequisite date, employment start date, projected validity period, and any later-referenced event must be consistent across the record.
- Track relationships across document types: a support letter, form filing, exhibit list, organizational chart, and employment letter should describe the same job and the same sequence without drift.
- If a document references another filing or event, verify that the referenced item exists in the record and that the reference is accurate.
- If one document corrects another, note whether the correction is explicit, complete, and consistent with the remainder of the packet.
- Treat inconsistencies in sequence or status as more than clerical issues when they affect eligibility, credibility, or the clarity of the response narrative.

## 6. Output structure conventions
- Produce a memorandum organized by RFE category, followed by independently identified issues from the petitioner’s documents.
- Use one consistent entry format for every issue:
  - Issue title
  - Severity
  - Relevant authority
  - Record basis
  - Why it matters
  - Response or correction strategy
  - Supporting evidence needed
- Define the severity scale once and apply it uniformly across the memo, using an ordinal ranking such as Critical / High / Medium / Low.
- For each issue, include a concise statement of the downstream consequence for the petition, response, or record integrity.
- Distinguish clearly between issues raised by the agency and issues found independently in the record.
- End with an overall risk assessment and a Recommended Actions block with imperative steps, the responsible role, and a timing anchor tied to the RFE deadline or other filing milestone.
