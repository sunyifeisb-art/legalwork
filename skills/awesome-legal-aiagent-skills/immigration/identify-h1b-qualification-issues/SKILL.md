# Skill: H-1B Qualification Review — Issue Identification Memorandum

## 2. Failure modes the skill is correcting

- Treating the petition as a generic resume check instead of separately testing the job’s specialty-occupation theory and the beneficiary’s qualification theory.
- Accepting a degree title without checking whether the transcript supports the claimed specialty through actual coursework, concentration, or equivalent depth.
- Accepting a foreign credential evaluation at face value without testing the evaluator’s basis, methodology, and fit to the H-1B standard being asserted.
- Reconstructing only the current petition period and missing earlier status transitions, gaps, or mixed-authority periods that can affect filing strategy.
- Conflating unauthorized work, lack of status, and unlawful presence; they are distinct and must be analyzed separately.
- Failing to reconcile the support letter, résumé, transcript, prior immigration records, and evaluation for internal consistency.
- Stating that something “needs clarification” without converting that uncertainty into a concrete pre-filing information request.
- Offering conclusions without anchoring them to the governing H-1B authorities.

## 3. Legal frameworks / domain conventions that apply

- H-1B qualification analysis requires both prongs: the offered position must qualify as a specialty occupation, and the beneficiary must be qualified to perform it under the statute and regulations.
- The core authorities typically include INA § 214(i), 8 C.F.R. § 214.2(h)(4), and the specialty-occupation evidentiary framework recognized in H-1B practice.
- Specialty-occupation analysis usually turns on the regulatory alternative pathways, including a degree normally required, industry practice, employer’s normal practice, and the complexity/uniqueness of the duties.
- Beneficiary-qualification analysis must examine whether the claimed degree is in the relevant specialty or whether an equivalent mix of education, training, and experience is adequately documented.
- Foreign degrees require a credential evaluation that explains the equivalency conclusion rather than merely announcing it; the evaluator’s qualifications and reasoning matter.
- Transcript review should focus on course content and concentration, not just the degree label; a related major may still be vulnerable if the specialty coursework is thin.
- Immigration status analysis must distinguish lawful admission, valid status, authorized employment, and unlawful presence; these concepts overlap but are not interchangeable under the INA and implementing regulations.
- Any history gap analysis should be tied to the petition strategy because unresolved gaps can affect credibility, work authorization analysis, and filing posture.
- If the record contains possible inconsistencies, the memo should identify them and recommend correction before filing to avoid accuracy and misrepresentation risk.

## 4. Analytical scaffolds

1. Position theory analysis: test the position against each specialty-occupation pathway and identify the strongest theory, the weakest theory, and any documentary gap for each.
2. Degree-and-transcript analysis: read the transcript as evidence, not decoration; identify the claimed specialty, the actual coursework supporting it, and the portions of the program that do not support it.
3. Credential-evaluation analysis: assess whether the evaluation states the standard applied, describes the underlying academic record, and explains why the degree or experience is equivalent.
4. Experience-equivalency analysis: where experience is relied on, separate job duties, duration, supervision, and contemporaneous evidence; do not accept résumé labels without support.
5. Immigration timeline reconstruction: build a chronological timeline from first relevant U.S. entry through the filing date; list each status, transition, extension, and apparent gap.
6. Status-gap analysis: for each gap or ambiguous period, ask whether the beneficiary had status, had work authorization, worked, or accrued unlawful presence; analyze each independently.
7. Cross-document consistency check: compare the employer letter, beneficiary statements, résumé, I-94 or other status records, transcript, and evaluation for mismatched dates, titles, duties, employers, or education claims.
8. Pre-filing clarification analysis: convert every material uncertainty into a targeted information request that counsel can use before finalizing the petition.

## 5. Vertical / structural / temporal relationships (only if applicable)

- Use a top-down structure: first the position, then the beneficiary, then immigration-history integrity, then cross-document inconsistencies, then pre-filing actions.
- Treat the immigration history as temporal and cumulative; earlier gaps can affect the interpretation of later status periods.
- When multiple periods, statuses, degrees, or claimed specialties appear in the record, enumerate them before analysis and address each separately rather than collapsing them into one blended narrative.
- If only one claimed specialty, one degree path, or one status gap exists, say so expressly and explain why no additional branch is being analyzed.
- Link each issue to the document set that creates it, so the reader can trace the concern back to the source materials.

## 6. Output structure conventions

- Draft a legal issue-identification memo, not an advocacy brief and not a filing cover letter.
- Open with a brief scope statement and an ordinal severity legend, using one consistent scale such as Critical / High / Medium / Low.
- Organize the memo in industry-conventional sections rather than copying any checker-specific checklist.
- For each issue, include: a concise issue statement, severity, governing authority, the factual trigger, the interaction with other source documents, the practical consequence, and the recommended resolution.
- Do not leave an issue at description only; every issue should end with a concrete next step.
- Use plain-text citations to controlling authority by name and section where possible; do not state legal propositions without identifying the governing rule.
- When multiple issues exist, present them as separately numbered items with clear subheadings.
- Include a chronology for immigration status and any gaps as a discrete section or appendix.
- End with a Recommended Actions section that assigns the action to a responsible role and ties it to a filing milestone or urgency level.
- Keep the memo self-contained, with enough structure that counsel can use it directly in pre-filing review.
