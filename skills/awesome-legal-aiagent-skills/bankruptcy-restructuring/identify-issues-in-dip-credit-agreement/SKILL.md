# Skill: Identify Issues in DIP Credit Agreement

## 1. Subject-matter triage

- Treat the assignment as a comparative issue-spotting review of a DIP package, not a clean drafting exercise.
- Read the agreement together with the budget, term sheet, motion, first-day materials, and any lender-side protections that affect economics or control.
- Separate core financing terms from process terms, because procedural leverage often hides in definitions, defaults, carve-outs, and budget exhibits.
- If multiple drafts or closing versions exist, identify which version governs and whether the documents are internally aligned before analyzing substance.

## 2. Failure modes the skill is correcting

- Agent spots a DIP lender conflict or claim-elevation device but does not connect it to the estate’s leverage, investigation rights, or committee timing.
- Agent flags a problematic term but does not compare it to market practice or explain why it is non-market in context.
- Agent identifies an issue without tying it to a concrete clause interaction, budget line, or downstream case effect.
- Agent omits severity labeling, making it hard to separate nuisance issues from case-shaping terms.
- Agent stops at diagnosis and does not propose fix language or action steps.
- Agent fails to detect that one document narrows or expands a term stated differently in another document.

## 3. Legal frameworks / domain conventions that apply

- Roll-up and claim elevation: Analyze whether prepetition claims are being converted into postpetition priority or enhanced collateral status; consider the effect on estate leverage under Bankruptcy Code §§ 364, 503, and 506, and on scrutiny of insider or affiliate claims.
- Investigation period and diligence rights: Test whether the estate, committee, or other fiduciaries have enough time and access to investigate liens, claims, defenses, and estate causes of action before rights are waived or converted; compare to common Chapter 11 DIP practice.
- Carve-out and professional fees: Evaluate whether the carve-out supports meaningful estate-side investigation and litigation activity, including committee and estate professionals, while checking for asymmetry if lender-side expenses are reimbursed more broadly.
- Avoidance-action and estate claim liens: Examine whether DIP liens or payment waterfalls reach avoidance actions, proceeds of litigation, or similar estate claims; analyze against Bankruptcy Code §§ 541, 544, 547, 548, 550, and 552.
- Defaults and control terms: Scrutinize MAC, event-of-default, exclusivity, and financing-market restrictions for subjectivity, overbreadth, or leverage that exceeds ordinary debtor-in-possession practice.
- Budget and fee mechanics: Check whether the budget exposes lender-side fees, premiums, and reimbursement items clearly enough to assess total cost and headroom; compare fee calculations to the stated commitment base and any rolled-up amount.
- Exit and termination economics: Review exit fees, early termination charges, and lender-default carve-outs for fairness, symmetry, and internal coherence.
- Confirmation and case dynamics: Consider whether the package shifts bargaining power in ways that impede competing financing, plan negotiation, or committee participation under Bankruptcy Code §§ 105, 361, 363, and 364.

## 4. Analytical scaffolds

- Review the package provision by provision, then cross-check each term against related definitions, exhibits, schedules, and motion language.
- For each issue, do all of the following:
  - identify the clause and where it appears;
  - explain why the term is problematic, non-market, or internally inconsistent;
  - tie the point to a specific dollar base, time period, exposure bucket, or other measurable metric in the source documents without inventing figures;
  - compare the clause to the surrounding document set and any other provision that changes its meaning;
  - state the practical consequence for the debtor, estate, committee, or other stakeholders;
  - propose concise modification language or a drafting fix.
- When a term can affect multiple parties, periods, or thresholds, enumerate the relevant items first and analyze them separately rather than blending them into one generalized critique.
- Treat missing disclosure as an issue when the package does not allow a reader to trace cost, control, or collateral effects from the agreement into the budget or motion.
- Treat inconsistent terminology, undefined capitalized terms, and misaligned cross-references as substantive issues if they change economics, rights, or remedies.
- Use controlling authorities where a legal proposition is stated, including Bankruptcy Code provisions, Bankruptcy Rules, and other cited authorities in the source set.
- If a proposition depends on generally recognized bankruptcy practice rather than a case-specific citation, identify it as market practice and state the practice anchor explicitly.

## 5. Vertical / structural / temporal relationships

- Trace any claim-elevation feature through time: prepetition status, effectiveness date, committee formation, investigation window, and postpetition repayment or priming.
- Track whether a default, consent right, or exclusivity loss is triggered by a filing event, marketing event, plan event, or budget variance, and whether the trigger comes earlier than market.
- Compare lender protections against estate protections on a mirrored basis: lender fees versus carve-out, lender diligence rights versus estate diligence rights, lender termination rights versus debtor termination rights.
- Check whether a provision in the motion, term sheet, or budget narrows or broadens the operative agreement text; if the documents differ, flag the hierarchy problem.
- Read temporal buckets in the budget carefully, because a fee, carve-out, or reserve that is acceptable over one period may be inadequate over the full case horizon.
- If multiple counterparties or stakeholder groups are implicated, assess each group separately before drawing an overall conclusion.

## 6. Output structure conventions

- Write a numbered issue memorandum with an upfront severity legend using an ordinal scale: Critical / High / Medium / Low.
- Lead with a compact summary table or issue list, then provide the body analysis.
- For each issue, use a consistent mini-structure:
  - Provision
  - Problem
  - Impact on Estate
  - Document/Authority Cross-Check
  - Market Comparison
  - Recommended Modification Language
  - Severity
- End with a Recommended Actions block that assigns an action verb, responsible role, and timing anchor for each major fix.
- Keep modification language concise and usable in markup or negotiation.
- Where the source set contains competing formulations, include the conflict and identify which version should control or be conformed.
- Do not bury severity in prose; state it explicitly for every issue.
- Do not rely on a single global conclusion when distinct provisions raise distinct risks.
