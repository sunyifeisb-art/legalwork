# Skill: Compare Competing Bid Terms Against Stalking Horse APA

## 1. Subject-matter triage

- Treat the stalking horse APA, each competing bid, the bidding procedures order, and any valuation materials as a single integrated record.
- Identify whether the task is a pure comparison memo or whether it also requires qualification analysis, ranking, and a recommendation; if so, cover each in the same pass.
- Enumerate every bid and every applicable comparison dimension before analysis begins; do not collapse multiple bidders or multiple deal terms into one representative review.
- Surface verbatim quotes from internal documents only when needed to anchor a contested point; otherwise paraphrase.

## 2. Failure modes the skill is correcting

- Focusing on headline consideration while missing bid certainty, assumed liabilities, closing conditions, fee stack, or process deviations.
- Treating a bid as qualified without checking every qualifying criterion in the bidding procedures order.
- Overstating financing quality by treating informal support, conditional letters, or uncommitted backstops as equivalent to binding funding.
- Ignoring how excluded liabilities, employee-related obligations, regulatory exposures, or similar carveouts can shift true value and closing risk.
- Missing timeline mismatches between outside closing dates, debt maturities, court deadlines, and required regulatory approvals.
- Failing to spot conflict-signaling terms, including management-retention conditions, bidder-affiliation issues, or other process-chilling provisions.
- Recommending the numerically highest bid without reconciling it against qualification risk and execution certainty.

## 3. Legal frameworks / domain conventions that apply

- Bid qualification is controlled by the bidding procedures order; a bid that misses a stated condition is not a qualified bid even if economically attractive.
- Sale-process comparison should distinguish headline price from net estate value after assumed liabilities, cure costs, break fees, fee reimbursement, and closing risk.
- Financing support must be assessed by enforceability, conditionality, source quality, and timing certainty; equity support, comfort letters, and binding third-party commitments are not interchangeable.
- Asset purchase structures must be read for assumed liabilities, excluded liabilities, indemnities, and any obligations that may survive closing by operation of law or order.
- Regulatory approvals, antitrust review, labor issues, and similar clearance regimes can make a bid infeasible even where economics look superior.
- A newly formed acquisition vehicle or thinly capitalized buyer entity warrants added scrutiny on closing certainty and operational capability.
- If the source record identifies any governing authority or local sale-process rule, use that authority as the controlling benchmark for the issue; otherwise cite the applicable bankruptcy-sale framework, court order, or recognized transaction practice in general terms without overclaiming.

## 4. Analytical scaffolds

- Start by listing, in numbered form:
  1. the stalking horse APA,
  2. each competing bid,
  3. the bidding procedures order,
  4. the valuation summary or other pricing benchmark,
  5. any other document that changes qualification or economics.
- Then analyze each competing bid in the same sequence:
  - Economics relative to the stalking horse and any valuation benchmark;
  - Qualification status under each bidding-condition requirement;
  - Financing quality and evidence of funding certainty;
  - Assumed versus excluded liabilities and any risk-shifting provisions;
  - Timing, approvals, and closing feasibility;
  - Bidder identity, capability, and any process-conflict indicators;
  - Net estate impact and recommended treatment.
- For each issue, close the analysis by:
  - tying it to a figure, threshold, term, deadline, or exposure from the record;
  - cross-referencing the other term, schedule, or document that changes the reading;
  - stating the consequence for the estate, the sale process, or closing certainty.
- Where a bid deviates from the stalking horse or procedures order, distinguish between a mere economic deviation and a qualification defect.
- If the source record contains a recovery benchmark or valuation range, use it to frame whether the bid is clearing, matching, or trailing the benchmark; do not invent arithmetic not supplied by the record.
- Rank the bids only after completing the individual analyses, and make clear whether the recommendation is based on price, certainty, or both.

## 5. Vertical / structural / temporal relationships

- Compare the stalking horse and competing bids vertically across the same term buckets so the reader can see where each bid is stronger or weaker.
- Track how one term affects another: a higher price may be offset by a broader liability exclusion, a later close may be acceptable only if approvals are straightforward, and a fee package may alter net value.
- Examine temporal sequencing carefully: bid submission deadlines, auction mechanics, cure periods, regulatory review windows, outside closing dates, and any debt-maturity or court milestones.
- If one bid’s condition depends on retaining personnel, approvals, or operational assets, assess whether that condition creates leverage or a hidden constraint on the estate.
- When comparing multiple bidders, preserve bidder-by-bidder consistency so that each is measured against the same benchmarks and the same process rules.

## 6. Output structure conventions

- Use a structured comparison memo with a short executive summary, a comparison table, bidder-by-bidder analysis, ranking, and a recommendation.
- Begin with a concise table that lists each bid against the stalking horse on the core deal terms the record supplies: price, assumed liabilities, financing support, closing conditions, timing, and any stated deviations from the procedures order.
- Use a clear severity label for each identified deviation or risk, with a consistent ordinal scale defined once at the top of the memo.
- For each bidder, include a compact narrative under standard subheadings such as:
  - Economics
  - Qualification Status
  - Financing Quality
  - Liability Profile
  - Timeline Risk
  - Process / Conflict Concerns
  - Overall Assessment
- State explicitly whether each bid is qualified, conditionally qualified, or not qualified, and explain the exact term that drives that status.
- End with a recommendation that identifies the preferred bid, the backup position if the preferred bid fails, and the estate action needed next.
- Include a final Recommended Actions block that gives imperative next steps, assigns each to a responsible role, and ties the timing to the auction, approval hearing, closing date, or other transactional milestone.
- Do not rely on visuals alone to preserve substance; any deviation, qualification defect, or comparison point should be understandable from the text alone.
