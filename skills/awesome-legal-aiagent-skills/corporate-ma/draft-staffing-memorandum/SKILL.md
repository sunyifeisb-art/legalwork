# Skill: Internal M&A Engagement Staffing Memorandum

## 1. Subject-matter triage

- Treat the assignment as a single internal staffing memorandum, not a client-facing deal brief.
- Read the deal materials, staffing inputs, conflict materials, budget inputs, and calendar inputs together before assigning names to workstreams.
- If the sources identify more than one jurisdiction, regulatory lane, or budget currency, enumerate each one first and staff it separately.
- If the sources identify only one relevant item in a category, state that expressly rather than implying additional coverage.

## 2. Failure modes the skill is correcting

- A proposed team member with a conflict of interest is included without identifying the conflict, checking whether the matter can proceed with safeguards, or planning a substitute.
- Specialty workstreams needing jurisdiction-specific regulatory expertise are assigned to generalists without confirming that the assigned lawyer or local counsel can handle the relevant approval, filing, or coordination steps.
- Client diversity and junior-associate commitments are asserted without verifying them against the proposed team composition using headcount and staffing-level calculations.
- Timeline risks arising from team availability, firm events, travel, departure, or calendar constraints are omitted even when the source materials show them.
- Budget impacts are stated at a headline level without translating rates into effective staffing consequences.
- A memo describes issues but does not convert them into staffing decisions, contingencies, or escalation points.

## 3. Legal frameworks / domain conventions that apply

- M&A staffing memos should organize by workstream, identify the lead lawyer and supporting team for each, and connect each role to a clear responsibility set.
- Conflict analysis should be framed against the applicable professional-responsibility standard and then translated into a staffing consequence: proceed, isolate, disclose, waive, or replace.
- Cross-border transactions usually require jurisdiction-specific coverage for foreign investment review, merger control, employment, tax, IP, real estate, financing, privacy, or other local approvals; each workstream needs a named owner or a documented coordination path.
- Foreign filing and clearance workstreams are time-sensitive and should be treated as discrete staffing burdens rather than absorbed into generic deal management.
- Diversity and junior-development commitments should be measured against the proposed team mix using the same counting convention throughout the memo.
- Effective rates should be stated on a discounted or blended basis where the inputs permit it; if the source documents give only standard rates, say so and avoid pretending there is an effective rate.
- If billing or expenses appear in multiple currencies, flag the FX exposure and describe its budget consequence in the memo.
- Relevant authority should be named where legal risk is discussed, including the applicable ethics rule, regulatory regime, or approval framework, rather than using unsupported conclusory language.

## 4. Analytical scaffolds

- Start with an explicit inventory of the staffing variables in scope: people, roles, workstreams, conflicts, specialties, timelines, rates, currencies, and diversity/development metrics.
- For each proposed lawyer or advisor, map three things: matter role, special expertise, and any constraint or risk affecting availability.
- For each flagged conflict, identify the applicable standard, determine whether the issue is disqualifying or manageable, and tie the answer to a concrete staffing adjustment.
- For each required specialty, confirm whether the source materials support in-house coverage, local-counsel support, or both.
- For each workstream, identify the lead, support, expected intensity, and any handoff or coordination dependency.
- For each diversity or junior-associate metric, calculate the proposed mix using the source documents’ counting method and compare it to the stated commitment.
- For each budget input, translate rates and discounts into the format used in the sources and note where assumptions are required.
- For each calendar constraint, identify the deadline it threatens, the person affected, and the mitigation path.
- For each legal proposition relied on, cite the controlling authority by name and section or other recognized citation form.
- Close each staffing issue with the practical consequence for the client or the matter, not just the problem statement.

## 5. Vertical / structural / temporal relationships

- Distinguish between matter-wide leadership, jurisdictional leads, and task-specific execution roles.
- Distinguish between primary attorneys, backup coverage, local counsel, and specialist advisors.
- Distinguish between pre-signing work, signing support, post-signing regulatory work, and closing/post-closing work.
- Where the sources show a sequencing dependency, preserve it in the memo: some reviews must happen before filing, some clearances before signing, and some staffing decisions before budget approval.
- If a team member is unavailable for a critical phase, identify the substitute by function rather than burying the issue in narrative text.
- If the matter spans different cost centers, rates, or currencies, separate them so the budget impact remains traceable.

## 6. Output structure conventions

- Single deliverable: a staffing memorandum.
- Use an industry-conventional memorandum shape, not a rubric-shaped list.
- Begin with a concise executive overview of the staffing recommendation and any gating issues.
- Follow with a proposed team summary organized by workstream, showing role, responsibility, and effective rate or rate basis.
- Then provide workstream-by-workstream staffing rationale, including specialty coverage and coordination dependencies.
- Then address conflicts, availability, and other risks, with a staffing consequence for each.
- Then address diversity and junior-development compliance using the same counting method throughout.
- Then provide the budget and FX discussion, including any assumptions needed to reach the estimate.
- End with a short recommended actions section that assigns the next step to a responsible role and ties it to the relevant transaction milestone.
- Keep the memo internally operational: it should tell the team who does what, what needs to be checked, and what needs escalation.
- Avoid filler, generic M&A boilerplate, and conclusory statements unsupported by a cited authority or a source-document fact.
